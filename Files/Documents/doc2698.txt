   <h2>
    Choose a Topic
   </h2>
   <div id="content_box_13943868" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      More Information
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       For more information on additional steps in the research and writing process, use the
       <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/aecontent.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=3856384">
        Find Information
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/aecontent.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=3878122">
        Research Tips
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/aecontent.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=3856388">
        Cite Sources
       </a>
       tabs across the top of this page.
      </p>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>
   <div id="content_box_13943674" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      Choose a Topic
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       <em>
        1. Identify an area of interest
       </em>
      </p>
      <p>
       If you have been assigned a research paper or project, there are usually a wide range of topics that you can select for your assignment. Here are some ideas for topic generating.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Examine the course syllabus, choosing a topic discussed in class.
       </li>
       <li>
        Identify a topic from course readings.
       </li>
       <li>
        Find a current event that relates to your topic through a newspaper or magazine article:
        <ul>
         <li>
          <a href="http://muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/login?url=http://www.lexisnexis.com/us/lnacademic">
           LexisNexis Academic
          </a>
         </li>
         <li>
          <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/">
           New York Times
          </a>
         </li>
         <li>
          <a href="http://www.sciencedaily.com/">
           Science Daily
          </a>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Type your subject into
        <a href="http://muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/login?url=http://search.credoreference.com">
         Credo Reference
        </a>
        , a collection of many reference sources, to find out more information on your topic.&nbsp; Your professor may not allow you to cite an encyclopedia article or other reference text as a scholarly source, but a good article can orient you to your subject, and you can find something that interests you to research.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <em>
        2. Narrow the focus
       </em>
      </p>
      <p>
       One of the best ways to&nbsp;narrow your research focus is to locate a point of ongoing scholarly debate or any element of surprise. These points of debate or elements of surprise may:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Be mentioned in reference books (see&nbsp;
        <a href="http://muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/login?url=http://search.credoreference.com">
         Credo Reference
        </a>
        or use print reference books in the library).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Appear in
        <a href="http://wikipedia.org/">
         Wikipedia
        </a>
        , by clicking on the "Talk" tab of a given topic.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Be&nbsp;identified by looking at differences of opinion between scholars in articles, findable in databases like
        <a href="http://muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/login?url=http://search.ebscohost.com/login.asp?profile=ehost&amp;defaultdb=aph">
         Academic Search Complete
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       For example, let's imagine that you want to write about the novel
       <em>
        The Golden Bowl
       </em>
       , by Henry James.&nbsp; You discover in some preliminary reading that there is considerable scholarly disagreement about the meaning of the golden bowl as symbol. Now you have a narrowed focus: the meaning of the symbol of the golden bowl.
      </p>
      <p>
       An advantage to identifying an ongoing scholarly debate or element of surprise as your narrowed topic is that it will likely generate interest on the part of the reader. You want to write about something that matters to your readers. Seek always to have impact.
      </p>
      <p>
       <em>
        3. Develop a research question
       </em>
      </p>
      <p>
       The next step is to extract a research question from your narrowed focus. This question will guide your research. If scholars are addressing a particular question, and have articulated that question, you could simply borrow that research question as your own. More often that not, however, a research question will need to be extrapolated.
      </p>
      <p>
       For instance, if you're writing about the symbolism of the golden bowl in the Henry James novel, you could ask: What does the symbol of the golden bowl really mean? Your paper could present the differing views on the controversy, and possibly go on to present your own educated guess based on careful research.
      </p>
      <p>
       Here's another example: Let's say you want to write about about Mormon polygamy, and scholars have focused time and again on the wives of polygamists, specifically their accounts of polygamy. You become interested in the accounts of Mormon women who speak favorably about polygamy as your narrowed focus. Asking how, what, or why can easily generate a question. What is it precisely that Mormon women found attractive about polygamy? Why did some women find polygamy rewarding where others found it to be insufferable?
      </p>
      <p>
       <em>
        4. Formulate a preliminary thesis
       </em>
      </p>
      <p>
       The preliminary thesis is the answer to your research question, based on what you uncover from your research.
      </p>
      <p>
       The thesis statement should have impact. It should state something that is not obvious, that may come as a surprise to the reader. The whole point of a thesis is to to look at a topic in a new and interesting way, to say what has not been said before. This may be difficult to achieve in an undergraduate research paper, but it a worthy goal to aspire to.
       <br />
       <br />
       <em>
        5. Refine the thesis
       </em>
      </p>
      <p>
       The thesis statement may need to be refined as the research process continues.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       Need more help with this?
       <a href="http://trexler.muhlenberg.edu/research/ask-a-librarian.html">
        Ask a Librarian
       </a>
       .
      </p>

   <div id="content_box_14009660" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      I Need to Search for Articles on My Topic
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       Databases permit you to search for articles on your topic from across many different journals, magazines, newspapers, conference proceedings, and other sources at the same time. These can help you find scholarly and popular articles, depending on the database. (See
       <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/content.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=4047073">
        What is Scholarly?
       </a>
       for more information.)
      </p>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Pick a Database
       </strong>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="http://muhlenberg.on.worldcat.org/discovery">
         Encompass Search
        </a>
        finds information by searching through a massive collection of books and scholarly journals, as well as newspapers and magazines, dissertations, conference proceedings, music, video, maps, and more.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <ul>
       <li>
        To find a particular database, check the
        <a href="http://trexler.muhlenberg.edu/research/databases-az.html">
         Databases A to Z
        </a>
        list. If you're not sure where to begin, an interdisciplinary database like
        <a href="http://muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/login?url=http://search.ebscohost.com/login.asp?profile=ehost&amp;defaultdb=aph">
         Academic Search Complete
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="http://muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/login?url=http://www.jstor.org/">
         JSTOR
        </a>
        , or
        <a href="http://muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/login?url=http://vnweb.hwwilsonweb.com/hww/jumpstart.jhtml?prod=OMNIFT">
         Omnifile
        </a>
        might be a good starting place.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <ul>
       <li>
        To find recommendations for subject-specific databases, try our
        <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu">
         Subject Guides
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <strong>
        Get the Full Text
       </strong>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Some databases provide the full text of articles online. Look for
        <em>
         PDF
        </em>
        or
        <em>
         HTML Full Text
        </em>
        links or
        <em>
         View Now
        </em>
        links.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <ul>
       <li>
        If a full text link is not available in the database, use the
        <em>
         Check for Full Text
        </em>
        button to connect to the article full text from another database or to locate the print/microform version of the journal in the database.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <ul>
       <li>
        If&nbsp;
        <em>
         Check for Full Text
        </em>
        buttons aren't available, follow the steps below in the "I Already Have the Article Citation..." section.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <ul>
       <li>
        If Trexler Library does not have online, print, or microform access to the article, request a copy of the article from another library with&nbsp;
        <a href="http://trexler.muhlenberg.edu/services/interlibrary-loan.html">
         Interlibrary Loan
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>
   <div id="content_box_14009661" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      I Already Have the Article Citation and Need the Full Text
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <ul>
       <li>
        If you have citation information for the article (i.e., journal title, article title, publication year, etc.), type the information about your article in the
        <a title="Citation Help Page" href="https://muhlenberg.on.worldcat.org/atoztitles/search?#article">
         Article Locator
        </a>
        on the library website. If the library subscribes to the full text online or in print, you will be directed to the article.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <ul>
       <li>
        If Trexler Library does not have access to the journal for the year/volume you need, request a copy of the article from another library with&nbsp;
        <a href="http://trexler.muhlenberg.edu/services/interlibrary-loan.html">
         Interlibrary Loan
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>

     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      Defining Key Terms
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       You may be wondering what we mean when we refer to
       <em>
        scholarly
       </em>
       versus
       <em>
        popular
       </em>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Scholarly:
        </strong>
        In-depth, research-based articles, books, and book chapters, written by scholars and experts and intended for other researchers, experts, and students.
       </li>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Popular:
        </strong>
        Articles, books, and book chapters written by journalists or professional writers for a general audience in common language (e.g., newspapers and magazines).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p style="padding-left: 30px;" class="MsoNormal">
       <em>
        Learn more:
        <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/content.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=4047073">
         What is Scholarly?
        </a>
       </em>
      </p>
      <p>
       While we're at it, how about a few more key terms...
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Empirical:
        </strong>
        Based on experiment or observation
        <strong>
         .
         <br />
        </strong>
       </li>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Literature:&nbsp;
        </strong>
        Scholarly articles, books, and book chapters published on a particular subject.
       </li>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Peer-Reviewed:
        </strong>
        A process through which some scholarly articles and book chapters are critically evaluated by experts with in-depth knowledge of the research area before publication in order to ensure the information is valid, credible, well-written, and of high quality.
        <span>
         &nbsp;
        </span>
        <strong>
        </strong>
       </li>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Primary / Secondary:
        </strong>
        See the
        <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/content.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=3874383">
         Find Primary Sources
        </a>
        guide. &nbsp;
       </li>
      </ul>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>
   <div id="content_box_10173314" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      Verifying Peer-Review
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       Are you looking especially for
       <strong>
        peer-reviewed
       </strong>
       articles?&nbsp;
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Many databases will tell you if an article has been peer-reviewed.&nbsp; You may even be able to use limits or filters in the database to narrow your search results.&nbsp;
       </li>
       <li>
        If you're still not sure if an article has been peer-reviewed, search the name of the journal in which the article was published in
        <a href="http://muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/login?url=http://ulrichsweb.serialssolutions.com/">
         Ulrichsweb Periodicals Directory
        </a>
        .&nbsp; The symbol of a referee's jersey next to the journal name indicates the journal uses peer review.&nbsp; (Refereed is a synonym for peer reviewed.)&nbsp; Note, though, that not all content in peer-reviewed journals is actually peer-reviewed; book or film reviews, letters to the editors, opinion columns, and articles without references are typically not peer-reviewed.
       </li>
      </ul>
     </div>

   <h2>
    --Locating U.S. Govt. Publications
   </h2>
   <div id="content_box_18797805" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      Locating U.S. Government Publications
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       Location: Trexler Library,&nbsp;
       <a href="http://trexler.muhlenberg.edu/images/levelA-map.png">
        Level A
       </a>
      </p>
      <p>
       At Trexler Library, U.S Government Publications are in a separate section on Level A. The publications in this section are grouped and numbered using the Superintendent of Documents numbering system (SuDoc Numbers). In Encompass Search this number is labeled as the "Call Number". For help with how to read a SuDoc Call Number, please refer to the tipsheet at the right or ask at the Lending Services desk or the Information Services desk.
      </p>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>
   <div id="content_box_18797765" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      Using SuDoc Numbers
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       <b>
        Tips for Reading a U.S. Government Publications
       </b>
       <b>
        SuDoc
       </b>
       Call Number
      </p>
      <p>
       SuDoc Call Number &ndash; U.S. Government Publications are organized using the Superintendent of Documents (SuDoc) Classification System. The Call Numbers are in a different order than other collections at Trexler Library. Here are tips for reading and locating a SuDoc Call Number:
      </p>
      <p>
       1.&nbsp; Documents are shelved first by letters and then numerically.&nbsp;
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        For example:
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; A 1.77:108&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
      </p>
      <p>
       &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; AA 1.2:C 76&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
      </p>
      <p>
       &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; C 55.2:D 87&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
      </p>
      <p>
       &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; CS 1.2:Z 9
      </p>
      <p>
       2.&nbsp; When reading a SuDoc number it is important to read each number between each punctuation mark as a whole number, not as a decimal.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        For example:
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; HE 20.40:
      </p>
      <p>
       &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; HE 20.108:
      </p>
      <p>
       &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; HE 20.3016:
      </p>
      <p>
       3.&nbsp; Look at the stem numbers first (before the colon).
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        For example:
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
       </i>
       HE 20.3612/3:&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Y 3.H 75:8 R 22
      </p>
      <p>
       &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; HE 20.3612/3-2:&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Y 3.T 22/2:2 G 28/3
      </p>
      <p>
       &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; HE 20.3612/4:&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Y 3.2:H 81
      </p>
      <p>
       &nbsp;
      </p>
      <p>
       4.&nbsp; Proceed with the remainder of the SuDoc number after the colon with letters taking precedence over the numbers.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        For example:
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; FS 3.64:R 4&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; HE 5.2:C 18&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
      </p>
      <p>
       &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; FS 3.64:46&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; HE 5.2:C 18a&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
      </p>
      <p>
       &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; HE 5.2:C 18/2&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
      </p>
      <p>
       &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; HE 5.2:C 18/3/2000
      </p>
      <p>
       5.&nbsp; Congressional hearings are shelved by House or Senate Committee
       <b>
        <i>
         and
        </i>
       </b>
       <i>
       </i>
       by the Congress &nbsp;and Session in which the hearing occurred.&nbsp; (Records will indicate the SuDoc number
       <b>
        <i>
         and
        </i>
       </b>
       the Congress and Session in which the hearing occurred.)&nbsp; Princeton File labels indicate the House or Senate committee SuDoc number
       <b>
        <i>
         and
        </i>
       </b>
       specific Congresses and Sessions. When looking for microfiche, divider tabs show the House or Senate committee SuDoc number
       <b>
        <i>
         and
        </i>
       </b>
       specific Congresses and Sessions.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        For example:
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Y 4.J 89/1:
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; U.S. Congress. House.
      </p>
      <p>
       &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Committee on the Judiciary.
      </p>
      <p>
       &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Hearings, etc.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 113
        <sup>
         th

        Congress,&nbsp; 2

   <h2>
    Find Primary Sources
   </h2>
   <div id="content_box_14010582" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      Find Primary Sources
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       <strong>
        On this page:
       </strong>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/content.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=3874383#14009717">
         Primary vs. Secondary Sources
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/content.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=3874383#14009718%20%20">
         Resources for Finding Primary Sources
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>
   <div id="content_box_14009717" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      Primary vs. Secondary Sources
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       In the humanities and some social sciences, a
       <strong>
        primary source
       </strong>
       is generally considered to be a document or object created in a particular time, providing contemporary accounts of events. Primary sources are original documents, giving first-hand perspectives of someone who experienced or witnessed the event being discussed.
      </p>
      <p>
       Examples of primary sources in the humanities and select social sciences include: letters, diaries, autobiographies, newspaper or magazine articles, photographs, advertisements and cartoons, speeches, paintings, interviews, research data and statistics, public opinion polls, etc.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the sciences, as well as some social sciences, primary sources are original articles or other texts&nbsp;describing&nbsp;a researcher's new experimental data, results, and/or theories.&nbsp;These primary articles usually contain an introduction, methods, and results section. However, some review articles (which are not primary sources) also contain these sections, so the reader should always review the article's content to assess it. An article can appear in a scholarly peer-reviewed journal and not be a primary source (e.g., editorials, review articles, book reviews).
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <strong>
        secondary source
       </strong>
       is an interpretation or analysis of the document, object, or event, at a later date. Secondary&nbsp;sources&nbsp;comment on or review one or more primary sources. Secondary sources are at least one step removed from the primary source.
      </p>
      <p>
       Examples of secondary sources in the humanities and some social sciences include: scholarly or popular books, journal article analyzing primary sources, documentary movies, etc. Examples of secondary&nbsp;sources&nbsp;in the sciences and some social sciences include newspaper articles summarizing an&nbsp;experiment&nbsp;and review articles summarizing many primary articles on the same subject.
      </p>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>
   <div id="content_box_14009718" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      Resources for Finding Primary Sources
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <div class="style4">
       <p>
        <em>
         The following resources will provide access to primary sources that are first-hand or original documents or objects created in a particular time, providing contemporary accounts of events. &nbsp;
        </em>
        <strong>
         <br />
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         AT TREXLER LIBRARY
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        Trexler Library has many primary sources in its collection. Search
        <a href="http://trexler.muhlenberg.edu" target="_blank">
         Encompass Search
        </a>
        to find these items. Include keywords in your search that describe the topic or event you are researching, as well as words that might describe the type of primary source (e.g., interviews, correspondence, diaries, personal narratives, sources, speeches) to focus your search. &nbsp;&nbsp;
       </p>
       <p>
        Trexler Library also provides access to some databases with primary source material.
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <a href="http://muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/login?url=http://search.ebscohost.com/login.asp?profile=ehost&amp;defaultdb=aph">
          Academic Search Complete
         </a>
         &nbsp;and
         <a href="http://muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/login?url=http://search.ebscohost.com/login.asp?profile=ehost&amp;defaultdb=ofm">
          Omnifile
         </a>
         &nbsp;provide full text of newspapers and magazines, mostly from the 1980s to the present. &nbsp;
        </li>
        <li>
         <a href="http://muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/login?url=http://poolesplus.odyssi.com/cgi-bin/phtml?ppsearch.htm">
          19th Century Masterfile
         </a>
         <span style="float: right; padding-left: 2px;">
         </span>
         provides citations to Anglo-American nineteenth century newspapers, periodicals, books, and goverment documents.
        </li>
        <li>
         <a href="http://muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/login?url=http://www.lexisnexis.com/us/lnacademic">
          LexisNexis Academic
         </a>
         provides full text of newspapers, magazines, broadcast transcripts, business publications, legal materials, and more, mostly from the 1980s to the present.&nbsp;
        </li>
        <li>
         <a href="https://muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/login?url=http://newspaperslibrary.proquest.com/?accountid=40980">
          Newspapers.com Pennsylvania Collection
         </a>
         provides full text of hundreds of newspapers from Pennyslvania covering the&nbsp; late 1700's to early 2000s, especially the 19th and early 20th centuries.
        </li>
        <li>
         The
         <a href="http://muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/login?url=http://www.accessible.com/accessible/preLog">
          Pennsylvania Gazette
         </a>
         <span style="float: right; padding-left: 2px;">
         </span>
         is an Early American paper of record covering 1728-1800.
        </li>
        <li>
         <a href="http://muhlenberg.idm.oclc.org/login?url=http://lado.alexanderstreet.com/">
          Social and Cultural History: Letters and Diaries Online
         </a>
         helps you locate thousands of private writings, personal narratives, interviews and more of people from diverse ethnic and social groups from 1550 to the present.
        </li>
       </ul>
       <p>
        See
        <a href="http://trexler.muhlenberg.edu/research/databases-az.html">
         Databases A to Z
        </a>
        for more or
        <a href="http://trexler.muhlenberg.edu/research/ask-a-librarian.html">
         Ask a Librarian
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        The
        <a href="http://trexler.muhlenberg.edu/collections/special-collections.html">
         Special Collections and Archives Department
        </a>
        of Trexler Library is the repository for the College's collections of rare books, manuscripts, maps, and ephemera, as well as material relating to the College's history.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         FROM OTHER LIBRARIES
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        Libraries around the country have primary sources, as well. These items may be available to Muhlenberg College students, staff, and faculty via Interlibrary Loan. Use
        <a href="http://trexler.muhlenberg.edu" target="_blank">
         Encompass Search
        </a>
        to search for them. Include keywords in your search that describe the topic or event you are researching, as well as words that might describe the type of primary source (e.g., interviews, correspondence, diaries, personal narratives, sources, speeches). Request items from other libraries using the Interlibrary Loan button.
       </p>
       <p>
        Local historical collections (like the
        <a href="http://www.lchs.museum/">
         Lehigh County Historical Society
        </a>
        ) often collect primary documents originating in the area and make them accessible to students and scholars.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         ON THE WEB
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        Many libraries, museums, historical societies, and other institutions provide digitized collections of primary sources on the web. Some noteworthy online collections and resources include:
       </p>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <a href="http://memory.loc.gov/ammem/index.html">
          American Memory
         </a>
         (Library of Congress)
         <br />
         American Memory provides free and open access through the Internet to written and spoken words, sound recordings, still and moving images, prints, maps, and sheet music that document the American experience.
        </li>
       </ul>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <a href="http://avalon.law.yale.edu/default.asp">
          Avalon Project
         </a>
         (Yale University)
         <br />
         Digital documents relevant to the fields of Law, History, Economics, Politics, Diplomacy and Government throughout history.
        </li>
       </ul>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <a href="http://padl.pitt.edu/index.php/index">
          Pennsylvania Digital Library
         </a>
         (University of Pittsburgh)
         <br />
         State-wide metadata repository for digital resources created by Pennsylvania libraries, museums, educational institutions, and other cultural heritage organizations.
        </li>
       </ul>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <a href="http://www.calstatela.edu/library/guides/psweb.htm">
          Primary Sources on the Web
         </a>
         (Cal State LA)
         <br />
         A listing of websites linking to primary source collections online.
        </li>
       </ul>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <a href="http://www.uiweb.uidaho.edu/special-collections/Other.Repositories.html">
          Repositories of Primary Sources
         </a>
         (University of Idaho)
         <br />
         A listing of over 5000 websites describing holdings of manuscripts, archives, rare books, historical photographs, and other primary sources for the research scholar.
        </li>
       </ul>
       <ul>
        <li>
         <a href="http://www.loc.gov/rr/program/bib/statememory/">
          State Digital Resources
         </a>
         (Library of Congress)
         <br />
         A compilation of state and regional digital projects and collaborations.
        </li>
       </ul>

   <h2>
    What is Scholarly?
   </h2>
   <div id="content_box_14692762" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      What is Scholarly?
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       <strong>
        On this page:
       </strong>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/content.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=4047073#14692740">
         What is a Scholarly Source?
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/content.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=4047073#14692744">
         What is Peer Review?
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/content.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=4047073#14692749">
         Why Use Scholarly Sources?
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/content.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=4047073#14692754">
         Finding Scholarly Sources
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>
   <div id="content_box_14692740" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      What is a Scholarly Source?
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       The following characteristics can identify a source as scholarly:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        AUTHOR
        <br />
        The author of the source is a scholar or expert in the field discussed, not just a journalist or "staff writer."
        <br />
        <br />
       </li>
       <li>
        CITED SOURCES
        <br />
        The source provides substantial citations, such as footnotes or a bibliography.
        <br />
        <br />
       </li>
       <li>
        CONTENT &amp; PURPOSE
        <br />
        The source discusses a narrowly defined topic and may review existing literature concerning the topic, present new research based on experiments or archival work, or interpret a well-researched idea in a new way.
        <br />
        <br />
       </li>
       <li>
        LANGUAGE &amp; AUDIENCE
        <br />
        The article uses technical language specific to the discipline, often requiring the reader to have scholarly background in the area.&nbsp; The source is intended for scholars, researchers, and students, rather than the general public.
        <br />
        <br />
       </li>
       <li>
        PEER-REVIEW
        <br />
        The source is reviewed and edited by other experts in the discipline.
       </li>
      </ul>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>
   <div id="content_box_14692744" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      What is Peer Review?
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       Peer review is a process through which some scholarly articles and book chapters are critically evaluated by experts with in-depth knowledge of the research area before publication in order to ensure the information is valid, credible, well-written, and of high quality
       <span>
        .
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span>
        Are you looking especially for peer-reviewed journal articles?&nbsp; See
        <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/content.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=3874364#14024403">
         Verifying Peer Review
        </a>
        .&nbsp;
        <br />
       </span>
      </p>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>
   <div id="content_box_14692749" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      Why Use Scholarly Sources?
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       Because scholarly sources are well-researched, written by experts, and discuss topics in great depth and detail, they have credibility and authority.&nbsp; Using scholarly sources in your own research gives your work credibility and authority, as well.&nbsp; Non-scholarly materials can also be useful and appropriate for some academic purposes, but generally scholarly sources are expected in academic research.&nbsp;
      </p>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>
   <div id="content_box_14692754" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">

     </div>

      <p>

      </p>
      <p>
       Need more help with this?

  <div id="guide_page_3878122" class="guide_page">
   <h2>
    Research Tips
   </h2>
   <div id="content_box_14024592" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      Research Tips
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       <strong>
        On this page:
       </strong>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/content.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=3878122#14024680">
         Too Few Search Results?&nbsp; Try...
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/content.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=3878122#14024682">
         Too Many Search Results?&nbsp; Try...
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>
   <div id="content_box_14024680" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      Too Few Search Results?  Try...
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <ul>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Breaking your topic down into its most important concepts.&nbsp;
        </strong>
       </li>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Instead of searching all the words in your research question (e.g.,
         <em>
          How does sleep deprivation affect college students' learning?
         </em>
         ), try using only the most important concepts (e.g., "
         <em>
          sleep deprivation"
         </em>
         and
         <em>
          "college students"
         </em>
         and
         <em>
          learning
         </em>
         ).
        </li>
       </ul>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Using broader search terms.
        </strong>
       </li>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Instead of searching very specifically phrased search terms (e.g.,
         <em>
          "sleep deprivation"
         </em>
         ), try searching more general or foundational terms (e.g.,
         <em>
          sleep
         </em>
         ).
        </li>
       </ul>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Adding synonyms for your search terms.
        </strong>
       </li>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Instead of searching only one word for your concept (e.g.,
         <em>
          learning
         </em>
         ), try searching multiple words that express the concept you are researching (e.g.,
         <em>
          learning
         </em>
         or
         <em>
          achievement&nbsp;
         </em>
         or
         <em>
          education
         </em>
         ).
        </li>
       </ul>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Using fewer search terms.&nbsp;
        </strong>
       </li>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Instead of searching every concept in your research question (e.g., "
         <em>
          sleep deprivation"
         </em>
         and
         <em>
          "college students"
         </em>
         and
         <em>
          learning
         </em>
         ), try removing some of the less important or more transferrable concepts (e.g., "
         <em>
          sleep deprivation"
         </em>
         and&nbsp;
         <em>
          learning
         </em>
         ).
        </li>
       </ul>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Adding variations of search terms.
        </strong>
       </li>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Instead of searching only one variation of a word (e.g.,
         <em>
          learning
         </em>
         <em>
         </em>
         ), try using truncation symbols to find variations of the words (e.g.,
         <em>
          learn*
         </em>
         will search for learn, learning, learned, etc.). (Note: most databases and catalogs recognize * as the truncation symbol, although a few use another character, such as !.)
         <strong>
          <br />
         </strong>
        </li>
       </ul>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Using a different search tool.
        </strong>
       </li>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Instead of searching only one database or catalog, try a variety of search tools.&nbsp;
         <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/welcome">
          Subject Guides
         </a>
         point you toward recommended resources in each subject area.
        </li>
       </ul>
      </ul>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>
   <div id="content_box_14024682" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      Too Many Search Results?  Try...
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <ul class="bullet">
       <li>
        <b>
         Narrowing your topic
        </b>
        .
       </li>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Instead of researching a very broad topic (e.g.,
         <em>
          role of the military in the Middle East
         </em>
         ), try narrowing to a more specific angle (e.g.,
         <em>
          role of the military in Syrian politics
         </em>
         ).&nbsp;
        </li>
       </ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Leaving out synonyms
        </b>
        .
       </li>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Instead of searching multiple words for the concept you are researching (e.g.,
         <em>
          politics
         </em>
         or
         <em>
          "political parties"
         </em>
         or
         <em>
          government
         </em>
         ), try searching the most specific, accurate term (e.g.,
         <em>
          "political parties"
         </em>
         ).
        </li>
       </ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Using subject headings or descriptors
        </b>
        .
       </li>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Instead of doing keyword searching, try searching with subject headings, also known as descriptors.&nbsp; Many databases and catalogs provide special terminology to more accurately and consistently describe topics.&nbsp; Using that terminology in the right way can find better results (e.g., a database may use the subject
         <em>
          "armed forces"
         </em>
         instead of
         <em>
          military
         </em>
         .)
        </li>
       </ul>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Filtering your search results.
        </strong>
       </li>
       <ul>
        <li>
         Instead of sifting through all the results your search turned up, try filtering your results by publication type (e.g., peer-reviewed journal articles), by date (e.g., only items published in the last 10 years), or by language (e.g., only items in English) to narrow to the most relevant information.
        </li>
       </ul>
      </ul>
     </div>
    </div>

   <h2>
    Cite Sources
   </h2>
   <div id="content_box_14306315" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      Cite Sources
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       <strong>
        On this page:
       </strong>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/content.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=3856388#14306337">
         Why Cite?
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/content.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=3856388#14306503">
         How Cite?
        </a>
        &nbsp;
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/content.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=3856388#14306525">
         What Is Plagiarism?
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/content.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=3856388#14306526">
         Keep Track of Your Research
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>
   <div id="content_box_14306337" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      Why Cite?
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       <span>
        Academic writing is filled with rules. &nbsp;Knowing when and how to cite your sources shows that you can take part in academic conversation and avoid any charge of&nbsp;
       </span>
       plagiarism
       <span>
        .
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span>
        <span>
         No professor expects you to present your own ideas only; be clear about where your ideas, phrases, and special words come from. &nbsp;
        </span>
        Any time you refer to another person's work--whether you quote directly from another's work, refer to another's work in your own words, or base your ideas on another's work--you must cite the sources you used. &nbsp;Citing your sources serves a number of very important purposes. &nbsp;For example, citing properly:
       </span>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Gives authors due credit for their work and ideas.
       </li>
       <li>
        Provides evidence of the depth and rigor of your research.&nbsp;
       </li>
       <li>
        Gives others a way to also find the valuable information you have used.
       </li>
       <li>
        Helps you to avoid plagiarism.
       </li>
      </ul>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>
   <div id="content_box_14306503" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      How Cite?
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       <span>
        Always ask your instructor which style of scholarly citation you need to use for a particular paper or project. &nbsp;See
        <a href="http://trexler.muhlenberg.edu/research/citation-guides.html">
         Citation Guides
        </a>
        for&nbsp;
        <span>
         examples of APA, Chicago, and MLA citation styles and more.
        </span>
        <br />
       </span>
      </p>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>
   <div id="content_box_14306525" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      What Is Plagiarism?
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       Plagiarism is the act of using another person's words or ideas without giving him/her proper credit.&nbsp; It is a serious academic offense.&nbsp;
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="http://muhlenberg.edu/main/aboutus/dean-academic/integrity/">
        Academic Integrity
       </a>

   <div id="content_box_19428314" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      What Is an Annotated Bibliography?
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       An
       <strong>
        annotated bibliography
       </strong>
       is an organized list of sources, each of which is followed by a brief note or "annotation." These annotations do one or more of the following:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li type="square">
        Describe the content and focus of the work at hand (usually a book or article);
       </li>
       <li type="square">
        Suggest its usefulness to your research or writing;
       </li>
       <li type="square">
        Evaluate its sources, method, conclusions, and reliability;
       </li>
       <li type="square">
        Record your reactions to the book or article.
       </li>
      </ul>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>
   <div id="content_box_19428327" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      How Do I Format the Bibliography?
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       Check with your instructor to determine which citation style is required for your class (e.g.,
       <a>
        APA
       </a>
       ,
       <a>
        MLA
       </a>
       , Chicago).&nbsp; See the
       <a href="http://trexler.muhlenberg.edu/research/citation-guides.html">
        Citation Guides
       </a>
       for information on citation styles. &nbsp;
      </p>
      <p>
       Then, remember that the bibliography is an organized list of sources. The annotation may immediately follow the bilbiographic information on the same line, or it may begin on a new line, two lines below the publication information. Since style manuals differ, check with your instructor about which one to use concerning form, spacing, and consistency.
      </p>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>
   <div id="content_box_19428350" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      What Goes into the Content of the Annotations?
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       Here are some examples of the four most common forms of annotations:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li type="square">
        <strong>
         Indicative
        </strong>
        : This form of annotation defines the scope of the source, lists the significant topics included, and tells what the sources is about. This type is different from the informative in that the informative entry gives actual information about its source. In the indicative entry there is no attempt to give actual data such as hypotheses, proofs, etc. Generally, only topics or chapter titles are included.
        <br />
        <br />
        Griffin, C. Williams, ed. (1982).
        <em>
         Teaching writing in all disciplines
        </em>
        . San Francisco: Jossey-Bass.
        <br />
        Ten essays on writing-across-the-curriculum programs, teaching writing in discplines other than English, and teaching techniques for using writing as learning. Essays include Toby Fulwiler, "Writing: An Act of Cognition"; Barbara King, "Using Writing in the Mathematics Class: Theory and Pratice"; Dean Drenk, "Teaching Finance Through Writing"; Elaine P. Mairnon, "Writing Across the Curriculum: Past, Present, and Future". (Source: Bizzell and Herzberg, 1991, p. 47)
        <br />
        <br />
       </li>
       <li type="square">
        <strong>
         Informative
        </strong>
        : Simply put, this form of annotation is a summary of the work. Begin writing it by re-stating the thesis; the develop it by condensing the argument or hypothesis, list the proofs, and state the conclusion.
        <br />
        <br />
        Voeltz, L.M. (1980). Children's attitudes toward handicapped peers.
        <em>
         American Journal of Mental Deficiency, 84,
        </em>
        455-464.
        <br />
        As services for severely handicapped children become increasingly available within neighborhood public schools, children's attitudes toward handicapped peers in integrated settings warrant attention. Factor analysis of attitude survey responses of 2,392 children revealed four factors underlying attitudes toward handicapped peers: social-contact willingness, deviance consequation, and two actual contact dimensions. Upper elementary-age children, girls, and children in schools with most contact with severely handicapped peers expressed the most accepting attitudes. Results of this study suggest the modifiability of children's attitudes and the need to develop interventions to facilitate social acceptance of individual differences in integrated school settings. (Source: Sternlicht and Windholz, 1984, p. 79)
        <br />
        <br />
       </li>
       <li type="square">
        <strong>
         Evaluative
        </strong>
        : This form assess the work's strengths and weaknesses. Why is the work interesting or helpful --or not? List what kind or and how much information is given --in short, evaluate the work's usefulness.
        <br />
        <br />
        Gurko, Leo. (1968).
        <em>
         Ernest Hemingway and the pursuit ofheroism
        </em>
        . New York: Crowell.
        <br />
        This book is part of a series called "Twentieth Century American Writers": a brief introduction to the man and his work. After fifty pages of straight biography, Gurko discussed Hemingway's writing, novel by novel. There's an index and a short bibliography, but no notes. The biographical part is clear and easy to read, but it sounds too much like a summary. (Source: Spatt, 1991, p. 322) Hingley, Ronald. (1950).
        <em>
         Chekhov: A biographical and critical study
        </em>
        . London: George Allen &amp; Unwin. A very good biography. A unique feature of this book is the appendix, which has a chronological listing of all English translations of Chekhov's short stories. (Source: Spatt, 1991, p. 411)
        <br />
        <br />
       </li>
       <li type="square">
        <strong>
         Combination
        </strong>
        : Most annotated bibliographies are of this type. They contain one or two sentences summarizing or describing content and one or two sentences providing an evaluation.
        <br />
        <br />
        Morris, Joyce M. (1959).
        <em>
         Reading in the primary school: An investigation into standards of reading and their
        </em>
        <em>
         association with primary school characteristics
        </em>
        . London: Newnes, for National Foundation for Educational Research.
        <br />
        Report of a large-scale investigation into English children's reading standards, and their relation to conditions such as size of classes, types of organisation and methods of teaching. Based on enquiries in sixty schools in Kent and covering 8,000 children learning to read English as their mother tongue. Notable for thoroughness of research techniques. (Source: Center for Information, 1968, p. 146)
       </li>
      </ul>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>
   <div id="content_box_19428361" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      Which Writing Style Should I Use?
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       The most important thing:
       <strong>
        annotations must be brief
       </strong>
       . Only directly significant details can be mentioned. Information apparent in the title can be omitted. Background materials and references to previous work by the same author usually are not included. Here are the three most common styles of annotations:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li type="square">
        <strong>
         Telegraphic
        </strong>
        : Write the information quickly and concisely. Be clear, but grammatically complete sentences are unnecessary.
        <br />
        <br />
        Vowles, Richard B. (1962). Psychology and drama: A selected checklist.
        <em>
         Wisconsin Studies in Contemporary Literature, 3,
        </em>
        (1), 35-48.
        <br />
        Divided by individual authors. Reviews the research between 1920 and 1961. (Source: Bell and Gallup, 1971, p. 68)
        <br />
        <br />
       </li>
       <li type="square">
        <strong>
         Complete sentences
        </strong>
        : The sentences must be grammatically complete, but their length can vary. Subjects and conjunctions must be present, but the tone must be terse. Avoid long and complex sentences.
        <br />
        <br />
        Kinter, W. R., and R L. Pfaltzgraff. (1972). Assessing the Moscow SALT agreements.
        <em>
         Orbis, 16,
        </em>
        34l-360.
        <br />
        The authors hold the conservative view that SALT can not halt the slipping nuclear advantage of the United States. They conclude that the United States needs a national reassessment of defense policy. They further conclude that the only utility of SALT is in developing a dialogue with the Soviets. This is a good conservative critique of SALT I. (Source: Strenski and Manfred, 1981, p. 165)
        <br />
        <br />
       </li>
       <li type="square">
        <strong>
         Paragraph
        </strong>
        : This forms requires a full, coherent paragraph. Sometimes the tone can be similar to the form and tone of a bibliographic essay. Obviously, this form requires complete sentences.
        <br />
        <br />
        Voeltz, L.M. (1980). Children's attitudes toward handicapped peers.
        <em>
         American Journal of Mental Deficiency, 84,
        </em>
        455-464.
        <br />
        As services for severely handicapped children become increasingly available within neighborhood public schools, children's attitudes toward handicapped peers in integrated settings warrant attention. Factor analysis of attitude survey responses of 2,392 children revealed four factors underlying attitudes toward handicapped peers: social- contact willingness, deviance consequation, and two actual contact dimensions. Upper elementary-age children, girls, and children in schools with most contact with severely handicapped peers expressed the most accepting attitudes. Results of this study suggest the modifiability of children's attitudes and the need to develop interventions to facilitate social acceptance of individual differences in integrated school settings. (Source: Sternlicht and Windholz, 1984, p. 79)
       </li>
      </ul>
     </div>
