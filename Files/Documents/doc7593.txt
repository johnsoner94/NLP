      <div class="trb_ar_page" data-role="pagination_page" data-content-page="1">
       <p>
        Pennsylvania leads the country in the number of confirmed cases of Lyme disease. And it's not even close.
       </p>
       <p>
        The Keystone State had 4,981 confirmed cases in 2013, according to the most recent statistics available from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention.
       </p>
       <div class="trb_gptAd trb_ar_main_ad" data-role="adloader adloader_ad delayload delayload_done" data-adloader-size="300x250" data-state="adloader_unloaded" data-withinviewport-options="bottomOffset=100&amp;topOffset=1000000" data-adloader-position="articleCube" data-adloader-adtype="articlebottomcube" data-adloader-label="data-adloader-label">
       </div>
       <p>
        The state with the second highest number, Massachusetts, had 3,816 cases.
       </p>
       <p>
        Why is Lyme so high here and how can you protect your family and pets?
       </p>
       <div class="trb_ar_cont" data-ar-cont="Article continues below">

       </aside>
       <p>
        We talked to Louise Bugbee, an educator with the Lehigh County Extension office.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         <em>
          Q: Why is Lyme disease so prevalent in Pennsylvania?
         </em>
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         A:
        </strong>
        We don't know for certain. Most probably it is a combination of ecological changes and environmental variations from year to year.
       </p>
       <p>
        One thing to remember is that not every tick is infected with the Lyme disease bacteria. Female ticks lay eggs, and the young that emerge are not infected. They acquire it from feeding on infected mice. As the ticks molt and grow, they feed on larger animals such as deer. The deer are efficient at moving ticks around but the mice are the reservoir for the disease.
       </p>
       <div class="trb_gptAd trb_ar_main_ad trb_ar_sntMobAd" data-role="delayload delayload_item" data-withinviewport-options="bottomOffset=100" data-load-method="trb.vendor.snt.init" data-adloader-position="sntbanner" data-adloader-adtype="sntbanner" data-load-type="method" data-adloader-label="data-adloader-label" data-adloader-size="300x250" data-v-snt="//content.synapsys.us/embeds/disaster/dynamic_mobile/partner.js" data-screen-size="mobile">
       </div>
       <p>
        <strong>
         <em>
          Q: Is there a time of year where Lyme is contracted more often?
         </em>
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         A:
        </strong>
        Peak tick season is May through October. But ticks can be active all year.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         <em>
          Q: What are the symptoms of Lyme?
         </em>
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         A:
        </strong>
        Most common early signs are fever, chills, headache, fatigue, muscle and joint aches, and swollen lymph nodes, alone or in combination. It is important to note that only about 70 percent of those folks infected develop a rash.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         <em>
          Q: How do I know if I've been bitten by a tick that is carrying Lyme?
         </em>
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         A:
        </strong>
        You don't. But we know that ticks in the Lehigh Valley area have a Lyme bacterial infection rate of slightly over 20 percent. That's significant.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         <em>
          Q: If I suspect I've been bitten by a tick, what should I do?
         </em>
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         A:
        </strong>
        If you develop symptoms but cannot or did not find the tick on your body, see a doctor. This is especially important for people who spend time outdoors gardening, hiking and fishing, for work or if you have a pet that might bring ticks onto your property or into your home.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         <em>
          Q: What are some ways I can protect myself and my pets?
         </em>
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         A:
        </strong>
        A tick needs to be attached and feeding for over 24 hours for the Lyme bacteria to be transmitted. That's why it is critical to check yourself, your children and your pets.
       </p>
       <p>
        Repellents are useful if you are out in the woods. There are products available for treating your clothing. Ticks do not fly, jump or fall out of trees. They climb up. Tuck your pants into your socks.
       </p>
       <p>
        Ticks are susceptible to drying out; they linger in the leaf litter, so try to stay in the center of paths. Create a &quot;dry zone&quot; between the woods and your yard to discourage ticks from migrating into your &quot;safe zone.&quot; And remember, mouse habitat is tick habitat. Keep wood piles away from the house and clean up garden debris.
       </p>

       <p>
        LYME INFO AND TIPS
       </p>
       <p>
        &#8226;In the Northeast, the tick most commonly associated with Lyme disease is the blacklegged tick.
       </p>
       <p>
        &#8226;Ticks search for hosts from the tips of grasses and shrubs and transfer to animals or people who brush against the vegetation. Ticks then usually attach themselves in hidden areas such as the groin, armpits and scalp but can also attach under watchbands and waistbands.
       </p>
       <p>
        &#8226;Lyme is often difficult to diagnose because the symptoms and signs mimic those of many other diseases or conditions.
       </p>
       <p>
        &#8226;Lyme disease is treated with antibiotics. Patients treated early usually recover rapidly and completely.
       </p>
       <p>
        &#8226;Blacklegged ticks are common in wooded, brushy places. Because new homes are often built in wooded areas, transmission of Lyme disease near homes has become a problem.
       </p>
       <p>
        &#8226;To prevent Lyme: Remove leaves and clear brush and tall grass around houses and at the edges of gardens.
       </p>
       <p>
        &#8226;After you've been outside, wash your clothes and inspect yourself and your children for ticks. If you see a tick, grasp it as close to the skin's surface as you can, and pull back straight with a slow, steady force.
       </p>
