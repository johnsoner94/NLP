          <div class="content-add05">
           <h3>
            Network Manager's Technote Spam / Virus Filter Information
           </h3>
           <br />
           <br />
           <h5>
            Summary:
           </h5>
           <p>
            This TechNote discusses the College's new Anti Spam / Anti Virus mail filter appliance.
           </p>
           <br />
           <h5>
            What it is and What it Does:
           </h5>
           <p>
            The College recently purchased a mail filtering appliance by Barracuda Networks which has been deployed at other LVAIC institutions and various other Colleges and Universities. &nbsp; The term "appliance" refers to the fact that this system is provided as a package of special hardware and software largely managed by Barracuda Networks. &nbsp;There is no software to update or maintain as with other solutions and the system largely does its job without requiring a lot of maintenance.
           </p>
           &nbsp;
           <p>
            Mail entering GroupWise and Hal are first filtered and scored by the appliance. &nbsp; The appliance looks for "unsafe attachments" which are used to harbor viruses, it finds viruses infected messages, and it scores the messages for spam and phishing attempts.
           </p>
           &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp;
           <div>
            <a href="/images/main/aboutus/oit/barracuda_th.gif">
             <img src="/media/contentassets/images/about/oit/barracuda_th.gif" alt="Information Technology" style="     " />
            </a>
           </div>
           <br />
           <h5>
            Virus Filtering:
           </h5>
           <p>
            The appliance looks for viruses. &nbsp;
            <span class="italic">
             If the appliance determines the message contains a virus, the message is logged and discarded before it reaches your mailbox.
            </span>
            This prevents viruses from entering your mailbox, making campus mail safer. &nbsp; The appliance uses at least two different commercial anti-virus&nbsp; products, like Sophos and McAfee, to scan your mail for viruses. &nbsp; This increases the likelihood that relatively new virus threats are filtered.
           </p>
           <br />
           <h5>
            Unsafe Attachments:
           </h5>
           <p>
            The appliance looks for "unsafe attachments." &nbsp; &nbsp;An "unsafe attachment" is an attachments which is commonly used to carry viruses but is very unlikely to be used for legitimate documents. &nbsp; Unsafe attachments include .exe, .pif, .lnk, and so on. &nbsp;
            <span class="italic">
             Messages with unsafe attachments are logged and discarded before they reach your mailbox.
            </span>
            &nbsp; We use the list of &nbsp;"unsafe attachments" maintained by Microsoft and feel that following the lead of Microsoft in this matter is prudent.
           </p>
           <br />
           <h5>
            Phishing:
           </h5>
           <p>
            "
            <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phishing">
             Phishing
            </a>
            " ( e.g. "fishing" ) is internetspeek for attempts to steal your identify or credentials or other personal information by using "
            <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_engineering_%28computer_security%29">
             social engineering
            </a>
            " and other means. &nbsp; Phishing attempts include fake notices from banks, e-Bay, and other services trying to fool you into logging in to a fake server with your password and user-ID. &nbsp;One you do this, you have provided the Phisher with your real password and login ID and perhaps other information like social security number. &nbsp; This information is then used for fraud or sold to others. &nbsp; So at its heart, phishing is about
            <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_theft">
             identity theft
            </a>
            .
           </p>
           <br />
           <br />
           <div>
            <img src="/media/contentassets/images/about/oit/phishing1.gif" alt="Information Technology" style="     " />
            &nbsp;
           </div>
           <br />
           <p>
            The appliance detect phishing by examining the mail for its intent. &nbsp; Is it trying to get you to login to known bogus web sites?
            <span class="italic">
             For obvious phishing attempts, the appliance logs and discards the message before they reach your mailbox.
            </span>
           </p>
           <br />
           <h5>
            Spam In General:
           </h5>
           <p>
            The appliance uses a spam scoring system to example the contents of a message. &nbsp; In addition the appliance checks the origins of the mail message to see if it comes from known spam sources or ISP's which harbor them. &nbsp;&nbsp; Obviously there are cases where one person's spam is another persons legitimate mail. &nbsp; Since the appliance does not contain a human brain, its not able to really read each message and "know" for a fact that it is or is not spam. &nbsp; It uses a &nbsp;very sophisticated scoring system which takes into account hundreds of different litmus tests. &nbsp; Its also uses bayesian analysis to do a better job of "understanding" what the content of the message is rrelativelyto our own norms.
           </p>
           <p>
            But no system will make everyone 100% happy. &nbsp; The nature of the scoring system is such the appliance categorizes mail into three categories: "probably not spam," "probably spam," and "yikes! &nbsp;that's absolutely spam all right!" &nbsp; &nbsp;For cases where the appliance is "conflicted" it will optionally tag the message - just like the old JSpamFilter system. &nbsp;
            <span class="italic">
             For instances where the message is very obviously spam, the appliance will log and discard the message before it reaches your mailbox.
            </span>
           </p>
           <br />
           <h5>
            Obvious Spam In Discarded Unilaterally:
           </h5>
           <p>
            The threshold for unilaterally discarding the spam message is extremely high. &nbsp; The appliance scores spam from 0 - 10, and the current discard threshold is set to 9.5. &nbsp; This value is higher than the value of 7.0 recommended by the vendor. &nbsp; However it is a "safer" value which prevents filtering of false positives. &nbsp; Some of the most horribly offensive spam scores higher than a 10, we have seen messages with scores higher than a&nbsp; whopping 26! &nbsp; &nbsp;
           </p>
           <br />
           <br />
           <div>
            <a href="http://brainiac.muhlenberg.edu/Technotes/Barracuda/Offensive1.gif">
             <img alt="Highly Offensive Spam" height="90" src="http://brainiac.muhlenberg.edu/Technotes/Barracuda/Offensive1.gif" width="100" />
            </a>
            &nbsp;
            <a href="http://brainiac.muhlenberg.edu/Technotes/Barracuda/Drugs1.gif">
             <img alt="Pharmacy Spamming" height="90" src="http://brainiac.muhlenberg.edu/Technotes/Barracuda/Drugs1.gif" width="100" />
            </a>
            &nbsp; &nbsp;
            <a href="http://brainiac.muhlenberg.edu/Technotes/Barracuda/Foriegn1.gif">
             <img alt="Foriegn Spam ( Asian / Russian )" height="90" src="http://brainiac.muhlenberg.edu/Technotes/Barracuda/Foriegn1.gif" width="100" />
            </a>
            &nbsp;
            <a href="http://brainiac.muhlenberg.edu/Technotes/Barracuda/Spamvertising1.gif">
             <img alt="'Spamvertising' - Using Spam to Advertise Otherwise Legitimate Products or Services" height="90" src="http://brainiac.muhlenberg.edu/Technotes/Barracuda/Spamvertising1.gif" width="100" />
            </a>
            <br />
            <strong>
             Caution: Above examples of actual spam material may be offensive to someone, I'm sure.
            </strong>
           </div>
           <br />
           <p>
            So you may ask, exactly what sort of &nbsp;messages are being discarded? &nbsp; Well, after examining log entries for nearly 2700 of these discarded messages, really an unpleasant job believe me, not a single one was legitimate mail, not even close. &nbsp; In most cases these were some of the most horrible spam messages imaginable. &nbsp; So we are totally confident that legitimate mail from any source will make it to your mailbox. &nbsp;However this leaves the matter of tagging.
           </p>
           <br />
           <h5>
            Spam Tagging:
           </h5>
           <p>
            The appliance currently tags suspected spam with "[JSpamFilter] [*]" for backwards compatibility with your existing spam rules. &nbsp; The appliance tags any messages with a score of 3.5 or greater. &nbsp; This is the recommended value provided by the vendor, and also is the value used by several other Colleges and Universities we have been in touch with. &nbsp; It strikes a balance between false positives and false negatives. &nbsp; Mail scoring below a 3.5 will not be tagged.
           </p>
           <br />
           <h5>
            Spam Tagging Accuracy:
           </h5>
           <p>
            I am sure that you have already receive messages which were spam and
            <span class="italic">
             not tagged
            </span>
            , as well as legitimate mail which
            <span class="italic">
             was tagged
            </span>
            . &nbsp; This is the reality of spam identification. &nbsp; There will be false positives and false negatives. &nbsp; However none of this mail is being dropped, you receive it all. &nbsp; So it is not, as it were, the end of the world if a message is inappropriately tagged. &nbsp;
           </p>
           <p>
            However we have been in touch with several people who have historically received huge mountains of spam, so much so that it is crippling at times. &nbsp;
            <span class="italic">
             We are talking &gt; 100 spam messages a day.
            </span>
            &nbsp;In each of these cases theses individuals have reported the tagging accuracy near 100%. &nbsp; &nbsp;For example one person went from an average of 70 untagged spam messages a day to less than 5!
           </p>
           <br />
           <h5>
            False Positives:
           </h5>
           <p>
            I am sure that seeing that "[JSpamFilter]" tag next to legitimate mail is irksome. &nbsp; For people who receive a lot of spam, they may use rules to automatically file these message in a folder for later inspection - this is the best way to handle larger volumes of spam. &nbsp; However there is the risk that a small number of those message might be important. &nbsp;However nobody is forcing you to automatically delete tagged mail. &nbsp; There is no excuse to later complain that "I never received that mail because you spam thing tagged it." &nbsp; It is up to the individual to decide how serious / debilitating their spam mail is and take appropriate action.
           </p>
           <p>
            In cases where you receive a small number of spam messages a day, the least risky way to deal with the problem is to use the tags as a hint rather than a litmus test. &nbsp; &nbsp;You can easily use the tags to quickly remove these messages from your mailbox in perhaps 30 seconds each morning. &nbsp; This way you will never miss a message even if tagged inappropriately. &nbsp;Its the safest and recommended way to deal with the problem.
           </p>
           <p>
            Some people receive huge quantities of spam and use rules to automatically file or delete tagged messages. &nbsp; If you want to do this, we cannot stop you. &nbsp; Since no &nbsp;anti-spam system is perfect, if you simply automatically delete tagged mail you run the risk of deleting legitimate mail, even mail from the campus community. &nbsp; The risk of this is very small, however you take on that risk by automatically deleting the messages. &nbsp; A safer route would be to have rules file the messages in a spam folder which you periodically peruse and empty out.
           </p>
           <br />
           <h5>
            Tuning Rule Based Handling of Tagged Mail:
           </h5>
           <p>
            As mentioned above, no system will be perfect. &nbsp; And you are likely to have those pesky listserves, daily new blasts, and other content you want to receive. &nbsp; You can easily tune you spam filtering / filing rule to make exceptions for these. &nbsp; You can add conditions to the rule to prevent mail from certain sources or with specific subjects from being deleted or files. &nbsp; Its easy. &nbsp; The support desk can assist you in fine tuning your GroupWise rules .
           </p>
           <br />
           <h5>
            Tagging - Win, Lose, Draw:
           </h5>
           <p>
            So the usefulness of the tagging scheme is mostly in the eyes of the beholder. &nbsp; For many people the tags are extremely useful, accurate. &nbsp; For others you may need to ignore the tags, or create loopholes in your GroupWise rules to ignore tags on messages you don't consider spam.
           </p>
           &nbsp;
           <br />
           <h5>
            Tagging of Mail Sent By Muhlenberg Community Members:
           </h5>
           <p>
            Because we are still in the middle of migrating people off our old mail servers and a number of other technical reasons, we do not have a means to tell the appliance "hey don't tag stuff sent by Muhlenberg people." &nbsp; &nbsp; Unfortunately about 20-30% of spam is sent with f
            <span class="italic">
             orged addresses
            </span>
            indicating the sender is @muhlenberg.edu. &nbsp; So we cannot simply allow all mail sent from an @muhlenberg.edu address. &nbsp; &nbsp;Today it is technically possible that something from "president@muhlenberg.edu" could be tagged as spam! &nbsp; &nbsp; The following section addresses this legitimate issue.
           </p>
           <br />
           <br />
           <img src="/media/contentassets/images/about/oit/too_many_recipients.gif" alt="Information Technology" style="     " />
           <br />
           <br />
           <p>
            The above message clearly has too many recipients., and was sent TO: Everyone Group rather than BC: Everyone Group. &nbsp; Every copy of the message includes a complete list of the hundreds of recipients. &nbsp; This makes each message very large. &nbsp; In addition, this message was also sent off campus to an AOL recipient. &nbsp; This fully discloses out entire address book to these 3rd parties. &nbsp; That's bad.
           </p>
           <h5>
            How to Avoid the Wrath of the Appliance:
           </h5>
           <br />
           Eventually we will be in a position to know precisely what is mail originating from Muhlenberg constituencies. &nbsp; Until then please use the following rules when sending mass / group mailings. &nbsp; These are basic rules to follow anyway.
           <br />
           <br />
           <ol style="margin: 0 0 0 25px;">
            <li>
             Address group / everyone mail using the BC: / BCC: field. This means "Blind Copy" which prevents the recipient list from being disclosed. Of you address these messages using the TO: or CC: fields, the spam filter will see you are sending to many, many recipients and will increment the score to the point that it might be tagged. Always use BC: / BCC: to address Everyone / Faculty / Staff or other large groups. This is a good thing to do anyway
            </li>
            <li>
             When sending mail use lucid subject lines, don't be cute or terse. Having a subject like "Important" is exactly the sort of thing spammers do. It is better to be specific: "Important information for faculty advisees." &nbsp; "Please see me" / "Look at this" is another favorite of spammers, be specific, e.g. "Please see me regarding spam filter technote." &nbsp;Many people see vague subjects and don't bother to open your mail - assuming it is spam even if untagged.
            </li>
            <li>
             Avoid sending spam. In some instances the spam filter will identify your mail as spam if it looks like spam.
            </li>
           </ol>
