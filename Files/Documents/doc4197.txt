        The laughs are long and loud at the Muhlenberg&apos;s production of &#8220;Avenue Q.&#8221;
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="trb_ar_dateline">
       <time itemprop="datePublished" class="trb_ar_dateline_time" data-dt="June 13, 2015" datetime="2015-06-13T00:00:00-0700">

     <div itemprop="articleBody">
      <div class="trb_ar_page" data-role="pagination_page" data-content-page="1">
       <p>
        The laughs are long and loud at the Muhlenberg Summer Music Theatre production of the Broadway musical &quot;Avenue Q,&quot; a sort of an adult homage to &quot;Sesame Street&quot; but populated by 20- and 30-somethings (both human and puppet) who are not quite sure where they're going in life.
       </p>
       <p>
        The hilarious show is profane at times, but hits its mark more often than not as the likable characters wrestle with issues of purpose, sex, love and commitment.
       </p>
       <div class="trb_gptAd trb_ar_main_ad" data-role="adloader adloader_ad delayload delayload_done" data-adloader-size="300x250" data-state="adloader_unloaded" data-withinviewport-options="bottomOffset=100&amp;topOffset=1000000" data-adloader-position="articleCube" data-adloader-adtype="articlebottomcube" data-adloader-label="data-adloader-label">
       </div>
       <p>
        At the center of the story is the well-intentioned Princeton, puppeteered by George Primavera, who sets off the tone right away with &quot;What Do You Do With a BA in English.&quot; Primavera's Princeton is sweet and sincere, and the actor, who is visible the entire show, does a great job of showing Princeton's conflicted emotions.
       </p>
       <p>
        Kate McMorran, who operates the puppet Kate Monster, perfectly captures the Muppet-esque quality of her character, who is eager and open, despite her disappointments.
       </p>
       <div class="trb_ar_cont" data-ar-cont="Article continues below">
       </div>
       <aside class="trb_ar_sponsoredmod" data-adloader-networktype="yieldmo" data-role="delayload delayload_item" data-screen-size="mobile" data-withinviewport-options="bottomOffset=100&amp;topOffset=1000000" data-load-method="trb.vendor.yieldmo.init" data-load-type="method">
        <div id="ym_1000189">
        </div>
       </aside>
       <aside class="trb_ar_sponsoredmod trb_barker_mediaconductor" data-adloader-networktype="mediaconductor" data-role="delayload delayload_item" data-screen-size="desktop" data-withinviewport-options="bottomOffset=100" data-load-method="trb.vendor.mediaconductor.init" data-load-type="method" data-vendor-mc="data-vendor-mc">
       </aside>
       <p>
        Especially funny are friends Rod and Nicky (think Bert and Ernie) operated by Luke Taylor and Sean Skahill. Taylor's Rod, a Republican investment banker who is determinedly in the closet, is especially comical, particularly if you watch his expressive face in tandem with the puppet's movements. Skahill makes the sloppy slacker Nicky appealing.
       </p>

       <p>
        Director Bill Mutimer and the entire cast deserve kudos for the skillful puppeteering. Some of the puppets require two handlers at a time, and it is amazing how smoothly everything goes. For example in a dream sequence, Nicky, who is operated by two puppeteers, and Rod who is operated by one, dance together in a complex mixing of legs, arms and bodies that somehow works perfectly.
       </p>
       <p>
        Trekkie Monster, the resident pervert who is obsessed with porn, is operated by Christian Dessler, but at times has two handlers. His scene in the nightclub is uproarious when he is overwhelmed by the charms of Lucy the Slut, operated with vampish glee by Morgan Reilly.
       </p>
       <p>
        The Bad Idea Bears are a pair of adorably cuddly puppets operated by David Forbes and Meredith Kate Doyle, who constantly urge characters to do the wrong thing, including spending their parents' money on beer. The two are hilarious as they cajole and manipulate in a recognizable little-kid way.
       </p>
       <div class="trb_gptAd trb_ar_main_ad trb_ar_sntMobAd" data-role="delayload delayload_item" data-withinviewport-options="bottomOffset=100" data-load-method="trb.vendor.snt.init" data-adloader-position="sntbanner" data-adloader-adtype="sntbanner" data-load-type="method" data-adloader-label="data-adloader-label" data-adloader-size="300x250" data-v-snt="//content.synapsys.us/embeds/demographics/dynamic_mobile/partner.js" data-screen-size="mobile">
       </div>
       <p>
        The human residents of Avenue Q include Christmas Eve, a therapist from Japan, played by Lily Randall, and her unemployed husband Brian, played by Dan Dobro. Randall is funny when he pokes fun at his wife's ethnicity, and the sight gag at the couple's wedding has to be seen to be believed. Dobro is lower-key, well-meaning and friendly but somewhat unmotivated.
       </p>
       <p>
        Mariah Dalton has a funny cameo operating the cranky puppet Mrs. Thistletwat.
       </p>
       <p>
        Cameron Silliman adds some sass to the human role of
        <a id="PECLB001072" title="Gary Coleman" href="/topic/entertainment/gary-coleman-PECLB001072-topic.html">
         Gary Coleman
        </a>
        (from TV's &quot;Diff'rent Strokes&quot;), who also is the building superintendent.
       </p>
       <p>
        The set designed by Curtis Dretsch has a &quot;Sesame Street&quot; vibe to it, with a New York street front. The entire show benefits from the intimacy of the Baker Theatre.
       </p>
       <p>
        The orchestra hidden underneath the stage nicely complements the character singing. Adding another dimension are video screens that sound out words like commitment, purpose and schadenfreude (a German word meaning pleasure derived from the misfortunes of others).
       </p>
       <p>
        Because of language and some puppet sex, the show is recommended for mature audiences.
