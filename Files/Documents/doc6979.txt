   <h2>
    Sources - Primary, Secondary, Peer-reviewed
   </h2>
   <div id="content_box_10140609" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      Defining Key Terms
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       You may be wondering what we mean when we refer to
       <em>
        scholarly
       </em>
       versus
       <em>
        popular
       </em>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Scholarly:
        </strong>
        In-depth, research-based articles, books, and book chapters, written by scholars and experts and intended for other researchers, experts, and students.
       </li>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Popular:
        </strong>
        Articles, books, and book chapters written by journalists or professional writers for a general audience in common language (e.g., newspapers and magazines).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p style="padding-left: 30px;" class="MsoNormal">
       <em>
        Learn more:
        <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/content.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=4047073">
         What is Scholarly?
        </a>
       </em>
      </p>
      <p>
       While we're at it, how about a few more key terms...
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Empirical:
        </strong>
        Based on experiment or observation
        <strong>
         .
         <br />
        </strong>
       </li>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Peer-Reviewed:
        </strong>
        A process through which some scholarly articles and book chapters are critically evaluated by experts with in-depth knowledge of the research area before publication in order to ensure the information is valid, credible, well-written, and of high quality.
        <span>
         &nbsp;
        </span>
        <strong>
        </strong>
       </li>
       <li>
        <strong>
         Literature:
        </strong>
        Scholarly articles, books, and book chapters published on a particular subject.
       </li>
      </ul>
     </div>
    </div>

     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <div class="style4">
       In the humanities and some social sciences, a
       <strong>
        primary source
       </strong>
       is generally considered to be a document or object created in a particular time, providing contemporary accounts of events. Primary sources are original documents, giving first-hand perspectives of someone who experienced or witnessed the event being discussed.
      </div>
      <div class="style4">
      </div>
      <div class="style4">
      </div>
      <div class="style4">
       Examples of primary sources in the humanites and select social sciences include: letters, diaries, autobiographies, newspaper or magazine articles, photographs, advertisements and cartoons, speeches, paintings, interviews, research data and statistics, public opinion polls, etc.
      </div>
      <div class="style4">
      </div>
      <div class="style4">
       <p>
        In the sciences, as well as some social sciences,
        <strong>
         primary sources
        </strong>
        are original articles or other texts&nbsp;describing&nbsp;a researcher's new experimental data, observations, results, and/or theories.&nbsp;These primary articles usually contain an introduction, methods, and results section. As a note, some review articles (which are not primary sources) may also contain these sections, so the reader should always review the article's content to determine what kind of article it is. An article can appear in a scholarly peer-reviewed journal and not be a primary source (i.e. editorials, review articles, book reviews).
       </p>
       <p>
       </p>
       <p>
        A
        <strong>
         secondary source
        </strong>
        is an interpretation or analysis of the document, object, or event, at a later date. Secondary&nbsp;sources&nbsp;comment on or review one or more primary sources. Secondary sources are at least one step removed from the primary source.
       </p>
       <p>
       </p>
       <p>
        Examples of secondary sources in the humanities and some social sciences include: scholarly or popular books, journal articles analyzing primary sources, documentary movies, etc. Examples of secondary&nbsp;sources&nbsp;in the sciences and some social sciences include newspaper articles summarizing an&nbsp;experiment&nbsp;and review articles summarizing many primary articles on the same subject.
       </p>
      </div>
     </div>
     <ul>
     </ul>
    </div>

   <h2>
    Getting the Full-Text of an Article
   </h2>
   <div id="content_box_10390246" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      How do I get the Full-text of my article?
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       To get the full-text of an article:
      </p>
      <p>
       - If you are in a library database with "Get-it" buttons, click on "Get-it." This button will take you the full text of your article or tell you that the full text cannot be found. If the full text is not available from Trexler Library,
       <a href="http://trexler.muhlenberg.edu/services/interlibrary-loan.html">
        interlibrary loan
       </a>
       the book or article.
      </p>
      <p>
       - If you have citation information for the article (title, author, etc.), type the information about your article into
       <a href="https://muhlenberg.on.worldcat.org/discovery">
        Encompass
       </a>
       . Find the article on the results list, and click on the title. If you have access to the article via Trexler Library, a link to full text or view-now will be provided.&nbsp; If not, you can request a copy of the article by clicking the "Request Item" button.
      </p>
      <p>
       - If Trexler Library does not have access to the full text of the article, you can also search
       <a href="http://www.google.com/scholar">
        Google Scholar
       </a>
       by article title to see if the author has made a copy freely available online. Look to the far right of the article's title to see if there is a link to a freely available copy.&nbsp;
      </p>
     </div>

   <h2>
    Cite Sources
   </h2>
   <div id="content_box_19686313" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      Cite Sources
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       <strong>
        On this page:
       </strong>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/content.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=3856388#14306337">
         Why Cite?
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/content.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=3856388#14306503">
         How Cite?
        </a>
        &nbsp;
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/content.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=3856388#14306525">
         What Is Plagiarism?
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="http://libraryguides.muhlenberg.edu/content.php?pid=471100&amp;sid=3856388#14306526">
         Keep Track of Your Research
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>
   <div id="content_box_19686314" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      Why Cite?
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       <span>
        Academic writing is filled with rules. &nbsp;Knowing when and how to cite your sources shows that you can take part in academic conversation and avoid any charge of&nbsp;
       </span>
       plagiarism
       <span>
        .
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span>
        <span>
         No professor expects you to present your own ideas only; be clear about where your ideas, phrases, and special words come from. &nbsp;
        </span>
        Any time you refer to another person's work--whether you quote directly from another's work, refer to another's work in your own words, or base your ideas on another's work--you must cite the sources you used. &nbsp;Citing your sources serves a number of very important purposes. &nbsp;For example, citing properly:
       </span>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Gives authors due credit for their work and ideas.
       </li>
       <li>
        Provides evidence of the depth and rigor of your research.&nbsp;
       </li>
       <li>
        Gives others a way to also find the valuable information you have used.
       </li>
       <li>
        Helps you to avoid plagiarism.
       </li>
      </ul>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>
   <div id="content_box_19686315" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      How Cite?
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       <span>
        Always ask your instructor which style of scholarly citation you need to use for a particular paper or project. &nbsp;See
        <a href="http://trexler.muhlenberg.edu/research/citation-guides.html">
         Citation Guides
        </a>
        for&nbsp;
        <span>
         examples of APA, Chicago, and MLA citation styles and more.
        </span>
        <br />
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       Need help with annotated bibliographies? Visit Purdue's
       <a href="http://owl.english.purdue.edu/owl/resource/614/03/">
        Online Writing Lab
       </a>
       for definitions and&nbsp; format of an annotation.
      </p>
      <p>
       <span>
        <span>
         &nbsp;
        </span>
       </span>
      </p>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>
   <div id="content_box_19686316" class="content_box content_box_1">
    <div style="display:block;">
     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      What Is Plagiarism?
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       Plagiarism is the act of using another person's words or ideas without giving him/her proper credit.&nbsp; It is a serious academic offense.&nbsp;
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="http://muhlenberg.edu/main/aboutus/dean-academic/integrity/">
        Academic Integrity
       </a>
       page defines plagiarism in more detail and provides information about Muhlenberg's Academic Integrity Code.&nbsp; Check out
       <a href="http://muhlenberg.edu/main/aboutus/dean-academic/integrity/plagiarism_defined.html">
        Defining Plagiarism: A Guide for Students
       </a>
       with helpful frequently asked questions.&nbsp;
      </p>
      <p>
       Indiana University's plagiarism tutorial also offers helpful information on understanding and recognizing plagiarism, including an
       <a href="https://www.indiana.edu/~istd/overview.html">
        overview
       </a>
       with basics and recommendations and
       <a href="https://www.indiana.edu/~istd/examples.html">
        examples
       </a>
       <b>
        . &nbsp;
       </b>
      </p>
     </div>
    </div>
    <div style="clear:both;">
    </div>
   </div>

     <div class="titlerow" style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      Keep Track of Your Research
     </div>
     <div style="margin-bottom:10px;">
      <p>
       Keep careful track of where you found your ideas, phrases, and key concepts so that you can correctly cite your sources. &nbsp;It may be helpful to use a citation management tool, like Zotero, to help you keep track of, organize, and cite your sources. &nbsp;
      </p>

       <li>
        <a href="http://www.zotero.org/" target="_blank">

        "Free, easy-to-use tool to help you collect, organize, cite, and share your research sources." Download instructions and help available on the site.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="https://www.zotero.org/support/" target="_blank">
         Zotero Guide (Muhlenberg College)
        </a>
        <br />
