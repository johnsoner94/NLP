      Muhlenberg President Randy Helm retiring
     </h1>

     <div id="mod-a-body-first-para" class="mod-morningcallarticletext mod-articletext">
      <p>
       Muhlenberg College President Peyton R. "Randy" Helm announced Saturday he will retire in June 2015 after more than a decade in the job.
      </p>
      <p>
       Under Helm, the Allentown liberal arts college's financial position strengthened along with its academic profile.
      </p>
      <p>
       Helm, who was named president in 2003, said he announced his retirement plans now to give college leaders time to conduct a thorough search for his replacement.
      </p>

     <p>
      "By announcing now and leaving in a year and a half, we have time to have a good transition, a smooth transition and happy transition," Helm said.
     </p>
     <p>
      With his 65
      <sup>
       t
      </sup>
      <sup>
       h
      </sup>
      birthday approaching and two cycles of strategic planning under his belt, Helm said the time is also right to put the college's plans for future growth and academic improvement in the hands of a new leader.
     </p>
     <p>
      "I'm just not going to be around to finish those things," he said.
     </p>
     <p>
      During his tenure, the college's endowment nearly tripled, growing to $200 million, and he oversaw a $110 million capital campaign, the college said in a news release Saturday.
     </p>
     <p>
      Muhlenberg undertook a major expansion of its campus, including the construction of a new life sports center, science building, residence halls; the expansion and renovation of Seegers Union; and the renovation of existing buildings for the performing arts, multicultural life, global education and social sciences.
     </p>
     <p>
      Applications to the college grew while admissions raised the academic profile of the student body. Muhlenberg also reported strides in diversity, with more than 10 percent students of color in the incoming freshman class for the past four years.
     </p>
     <p>
      "I think the strategic plan resulted in us being stronger academically, stronger financially and being stronger in the marketplace," Helm said.
     </p>
     <p>
      With 17 months left before he sends off his final graduating class, Helm said he has much work to do completing the implementation of a new curriculum, a summer program for entering business students and assisting in the search.
     </p>
     <p>
      "It's not going to be a victory lap so much as a sprint to the finish," Helm said.
     </p>
     <p>
      He also looks forward to teaching his ancient Greek history and Homeric epics course again.
     </p>
     <p>
      "My job has a lot of fun parts, but that's the most fun," he said.
     </p>
     <p>
      Known for his brightly colored bow ties and large white dog, Calypso, that he walked on campus each morning, Helm made an impression as a highly visible leader who was accessible to students, said those out on Muhlenbreg's campus Saturday.
     </p>
     <p>
      Madeline Tremont, a freshman from Rehoboth, Mass., said she was sad to hear Helm will be leaving. Tremont knows him as a fun and friendly administrator who was at ease sitting down in the dining hall to eat with students.
     </p>
     <p>
      Mari Anne Skolnik, a senior from Brick, N.J., said Helm would dress as pop culture icons each year for freshman orientation. When Skolnik was a freshman, Helm came as Dumbledore, the headmaster of the wizarding school Hogwarts in the Harry Potter series. This year, he was Batman, students said.
     </p>

      Helm is Muhlenberg's 11th president. He replaced Arthur Taylor, a controversial figure who had led the college for a decade.
     </p>
     <p>
      Helm came to Muhlenberg after being vice president for college relations and a professor of classics at Colby College in Waterville, Maine.
     </p>
     <p>
      He is the latest in a string of Lehigh Valley college presidents to leave their positions in recent years.
     </p>
     <p>
      Last month, Lehigh University President Alice Gast was named president of Imperial College in London, while Kutztown University President F. Javier Cevallos was selected to lead Framingham State University in the Boston area.
     </p>
     <p>
      Northampton Community College inaugurated new President Mark Erickson in 2012. Lafayette College hired President Alison R. Byerly in 2013, Moravian College named Bryon L. Grigsby president in 2013 and Lehigh Carbon Community College will hire a new president this year to replace Donald Snyder, who retired at the end of 2013.
     </p>
     <p>
      With the exception of DeSales University President the Rev. Bernard O'Connor, Helm is the longest serving president in the Lehigh Valley.
