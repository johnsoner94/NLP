So, this is my second post about the mainstage Salome, and things are undergoing some pretty significant changes.
A week or so ago, we had our first designer run, and decided to redo and revamp
some of the scenes. We had Beth Schachter sit in on
some of our rehearsals and play status games with us, so that the cast could
get acquainted with the idea of power. Since the show plays with such
power-heavy scenes, the establishment was necessary. Not to mention the really
interesting power dynamics between characters, some as stark as queen to
servant. Playing with status is helpful to establish relationships with
characters you interact mostly with. The only character in which I have
dialogue with is Salome, and exploring the tension between a princess and a
prophet is very complicated, because one is important on the earthly realm,
which is respected by her subordinates, while the other is a vessel of God, but
is not necessarily respected by those around her. Interacting with the
different actors on stage while playing such a high status is really intriguing
because their reaction is indicative of how high my status is. It affects the
way I hold myself, the way I speak, the fluidity of my motion, etc. Exploring
these differences attunes you to those around you, which is so incredibly helpful
in establishing a character, and its significance. That&rsquo;s not to say that only
those of high status matter in a play. The reality of a production stems from
the reactions of all characters with each other, regardless of status. Status
merely acts as a guideline so that a servant doesn&rsquo;t challenge someone of a
higher status unjustly, or a high status character is inconsistent in their
treatment of those below them. But competing with someone of the same status is
where things really get interesting. The time in which you feel out your sense
of power is different; it&rsquo;s slower and more deliberate. You&rsquo;re more cautious of
your actions, and how the other person is receiving them. I still don&rsquo;t have
everything figured out about the status in Salome,
but I really look forward to continuing the push-and-pull process." />

        <h2>
         Discovering the Power Dynamic
        </h2>
        <p>
         Hello!
So, this is my second post about the mainstage
         <i>
          Salome,
         </i>
         and things are undergoing some pretty significant changes.
A week or so ago, we had our first designer run, and decided to redo and revamp
some of the scenes. We had Beth Schachter sit in on
some of our rehearsals and play status games with us, so that the cast could
get acquainted with the idea of power. Since the show plays with such
power-heavy scenes, the establishment was necessary. Not to mention the really
interesting power dynamics between characters, some as stark as queen to
servant. Playing with status is helpful to establish relationships with
characters you interact mostly with. The only character in which I have
dialogue with is Salome, and exploring the tension between a princess and a
prophet is very complicated, because one is important on the earthly realm,
which is respected by her subordinates, while the other is a vessel of God, but
is not necessarily respected by those around her. Interacting with the
different actors on stage while playing such a high status is really intriguing
because their reaction is indicative of how high my status is. It affects the
way I hold myself, the way I speak, the fluidity of my motion, etc. Exploring
these differences attunes you to those around you, which is so incredibly helpful
in establishing a character, and its significance. That&rsquo;s not to say that only
those of high status matter in a play. The reality of a production stems from
the reactions of all characters with each other, regardless of status. Status
merely acts as a guideline so that a servant doesn&rsquo;t challenge someone of a
higher status unjustly, or a high status character is inconsistent in their
treatment of those below them. But competing with someone of the same status is
where things really get interesting. The time in which you feel out your sense
of power is different; it&rsquo;s slower and more deliberate. You&rsquo;re more cautious of
your actions, and how the other person is receiving them. I still don&rsquo;t have
everything figured out about the status in
         <i>
          Salome
         </i>
         ,
but I really look forward to continuing the push-and-pull process.
        </p>
