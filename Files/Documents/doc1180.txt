        <h2>
         <a href="http://muhlenbergtheatredance.tumblr.com/post/153778245426/a-well-deserved-break" title="A Well-Deserved Break">
          A Well-Deserved Break
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         Blog post #4, and Happy Thanksgiving! The cast of
         <i>
          Salome
         </i>
         is currently enjoying a
well-deserved break, but after a remarkably productive first weekend of tech.
Honestly, I am so amazed at how much more a show comes together at the
introduction of a set, lighting, and sound, and our first costume-run is on
Sunday. The decadence of the set, and the elaborateness of the scenic design
has really stream-lined the whole process, and the transition from the
rehearsal room to the stage has been great. The rake of the stage has taken
some getting used to, and some reworking of the blocking, but after last
Sunday, we&rsquo;ve found just how much of a show we have on our hands. I believe
just recently we surpassed fifty rehearsals, which attests just how much work
has gone into this one-act. It&rsquo;s wild to think that
         <i>
          Salome
         </i>
         opens in less than a week now, but given that almost all of
the tech work is done, we&rsquo;ve all got our fingers crossed for the best of runs.
I know this post is a little shorter than the ones in the past, but anticipate
my fifth and final post post-opening. Otherwise, the long hours of tech are
going well, productivity is at an all-time high, and
         <i>
          Salome
         </i>
         is going to be a show with wonderful and relevant subtext,
and performed beautifully by an array of fabulously empowered women. Buy your
tickets, we open Wednesday, November 30!
        </p>
        <p>
         .
        </p>

        <h2>
         <a href="http://muhlenbergtheatredance.tumblr.com/post/153009056056/the-process" title="The Process">
          The Process
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         Here goes my third blog
post! As
         <i>
          Salome
         </i>
         runs about 2 weeks
from tech, things are stringing together gradually, but progress is being made.
As things are becoming more detailed, things have begun to move slower, but
with more attention to ensemble work, and specificity of motion. Beth Schachter
has been sitting in on our rehearsals lately, and has been a firm advocate for
us to all expand our arms, and take up space, as corresponding to our
respective characters. While at first the focus on arms felt awkward and
over-the-top, we have all conclusively found that the usage and mobility of our
full body expands the status and significance of our motions and
characterizations. I do wish we could expedite the process so that the final
product would be visible ASAP, but the process is probably the most beneficial
aspect of it, however it seems so gruesome right now. The nit-picky repetition
is a little hard, but hopefully the end product will be something I am proud to
be a part of. As a cast, we do have a habit of distraction, which can be fun,
but also a bit taxing at points, because it elongates the fluidity and flow of
things, which we&rsquo;re sort of running out of time to do.
         <i>
          Salome
         </i>
         as a whole is a very ambitious project, what with the
heightened text and usage of an ensemble, but the sense of camaraderie is a
really nice aspect of it. However, I do wish currently we were buckling down
and paying attention to the text, and were truly present during rehearsals, so
as to capitalize on the four hour rehearsals we endure. There is a little bit
of frustration joined with this part of the process, which is the real testing
point of whether the show is going to come together or not. However, I still
think we have a strong show on our hands, we just need to regroup, and really
commit to characterization, text, and status/relationships. Progress, albeit
hard, is being made.
        </p>

         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         Hi guys, it&rsquo;s Evan Plaza back at it again with another blog post! &nbsp;So we have just closed our Mainstage Production of The Pirates of Penzance. &nbsp;We have put on this show 8 times, from October 28
         <sup>
          th
         </sup>
         to November 6
         <sup>
          th
         </sup>
         . &nbsp;I am not gonna lie, it seems weird to now have free time in my schedule again. &nbsp;I felt like after we had closed the show, I was thinking about the next day when we had to go to call time tomorrow and get ready. &nbsp;This show was my first Mainstage production, and I felt like I have learned so much from this experience. &nbsp;I have learned what it is like to rehearse a show in a month and a half&rsquo;s worth of time, and how valuable that time is to the production. &nbsp;The rehearsal process is so special to any production because it is where the actors get to play and the show begins to mold itself together like a refined sculpture. &nbsp;Having this time determines many things about a show: the tone, the flow, and can even define the overall message that the show is trying to portray. &nbsp;During this process I also learned an essential skill that every actor, better yet, every person, should learn: how to be professional. &nbsp;This show required an amount of time to put together, the time needed to explore and create art determines the success of the show. &nbsp;Having said that, it is important to be punctual, and come prepared to create the art. &nbsp;It is also important to be patient and to come with different ideas to the show; some choices might not fit with the director&rsquo;s vision, or just don&rsquo;t correlate with the characters in the show. &nbsp;I am so grateful I got the opportunity to work on this production alongside a loving and supporting cast. &nbsp;That&rsquo;s all for now, I&rsquo;ll see you next time!

        <h2>
         <a href="http://muhlenbergtheatredance.tumblr.com/post/152258913006/discovering-the-power-dynamic" title="Discovering the Power Dynamic">
          Discovering the Power Dynamic
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         Hello!
So, this is my second post about the mainstage
         <i>
          Salome,
         </i>
         and things are undergoing some pretty significant changes.
A week or so ago, we had our first designer run, and decided to redo and revamp
some of the scenes. We had Beth Schachter sit in on
some of our rehearsals and play status games with us, so that the cast could
get acquainted with the idea of power. Since the show plays with such
power-heavy scenes, the establishment was necessary. Not to mention the really
interesting power dynamics between characters, some as stark as queen to
servant. Playing with status is helpful to establish relationships with
characters you interact mostly with. The only character in which I have
dialogue with is Salome, and exploring the tension between a princess and a
prophet is very complicated, because one is important on the earthly realm,
which is respected by her subordinates, while the other is a vessel of God, but
is not necessarily respected by those around her. Interacting with the
different actors on stage while playing such a high status is really intriguing
because their reaction is indicative of how high my status is. It affects the
way I hold myself, the way I speak, the fluidity of my motion, etc. Exploring
these differences attunes you to those around you, which is so incredibly helpful
in establishing a character, and its significance. That&rsquo;s not to say that only
those of high status matter in a play. The reality of a production stems from
the reactions of all characters with each other, regardless of status. Status
merely acts as a guideline so that a servant doesn&rsquo;t challenge someone of a
higher status unjustly, or a high status character is inconsistent in their
treatment of those below them. But competing with someone of the same status is
where things really get interesting. The time in which you feel out your sense
of power is different; it&rsquo;s slower and more deliberate. You&rsquo;re more cautious of
your actions, and how the other person is receiving them. I still don&rsquo;t have
everything figured out about the status in
         <i>
          Salome
         </i>
         ,
but I really look forward to continuing the push-and-pull process.
        </p>

          Empowering Females
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         So, I guess I should start off with a sort of
introduction. My name is Julia, I&rsquo;m a sophomore at Muhlenberg College, and I am
playing Iokanaan in the upcoming production of
         <i>
          Salome
         </i>
         by Oscar Wilde, a part of the second set of New Visions
festival, Falling. We&rsquo;ve been in rehearsal for a little over a month now, and
honestly, I&rsquo;m beyond excited about the cast, the process, and working with a
student director on a Mainstage production. Since the show is gender-bent, and
consists entirely of girls, the dynamic of the rehearsal process is actually
very different from any experiences that I&rsquo;ve had in the past. Personally,
given the sexual connotations of the piece, it&rsquo;s actually been pretty amazing
working with a group of extremely empowered, fluid actresses who are very
supportive of one another. Since the entire cast is comprised of women, the
idea of sexuality isn&rsquo;t projected as a hindrance, but instead, it&rsquo;s more used
as an empowering reclamation of the female form.
         <i>
          Salome
         </i>
         is movement-based, and watching my cast mates partake in a
powerful, highly sexual dance is actually really remarkable. There&rsquo;s no real
strive to fulfill the male gaze in rehearsal; the work is entirely that of the
actresses. It even alleviates some of the stress of appearing &ldquo;sexy&rdquo; on stage,
because the movement is so freeing and strong, really reiterating the concept
that sexuality is not dependent on the perception of men, but instead a means
of self-identity and establishment. The movement of the female form is that of
grace and poise, not of showcasing its assets. I&rsquo;ll be submitting posts on a
roughly 2 week basis, so stay tuned!
        </p>

         <br />
         What have been the biggest challenges for me are the multiple characters I play in the show. &nbsp;Oh? &nbsp;Did I not mention? &nbsp;There&rsquo;s three people in the cast and 7 characters. &nbsp;I play Nell Gwynne, a brilliantly witty but foul-mouthed actress, a cantankerous servant (Maria) and a very wealthy and quite possibly unhinged patron of the arts (Lady Davenport). &nbsp; It&rsquo;s tough giving each character a different posture, age, walk, and voice, but that&rsquo;s what also makes it so fun. &nbsp;One of the highest compliments I get after a show is when an audience member tells me they had no idea that I played all three women. &nbsp;It means I&rsquo;ve done my job well. &nbsp;

        <h2>
         <a href="http://muhlenbergtheatredance.tumblr.com/post/132677029821/chicago-onstage-to-tech" title="Chicago: Onstage to Tech">
          Chicago: Onstage to Tech
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         OCT. 20: FIRST RUN
        </p>
        <p>
         The first full run-through of the show was a lot of fun! It feels amazing to be part of an experience where everyone involved is so on top of material they are conquering. It also feels great to know that our rehearsals have gotten to the nit-picky technical stage, so far in advance of tech week; it means the show is in great shape! I actually think this tech week will concentrate less on the movement we do, and more on the technical side of the production. There will be scenes that need to be spaced out and repositioned, but those are expected and easy fixes.
        </p>
        <p>
         We finished staging one of the numbers, &ldquo;All I Care About is Love,&rdquo; just before the first run-through. We have now begun to clean it up and shape it to best fit the group of dynamic actors and dancers. It&rsquo;s fun to experiment with ways in which this part of the show can be as interesting as possible. I&rsquo;m sure that, when audiences see the show, this number will be unforgettable. I am excited to continue cleaning tonight, as I will be at rehearsal from 7:45 to 11 pm. Tonight is a basic brush-up of major numbers, and I&rsquo;m excited to work with choreographer Jeffrey Peterson on some of my favorite parts of the show!
        </p>
        <p>
         OCT. 23: ON STAGE
        </p>
        <p>
         We held our first on-stage rehearsal tonight! That means this was the first time we were rehearsing in the actual space where our show is being held. The Empie Theatre looks fantastic, and when my castmates and I got there, we were in absolute awe of how gorgeous the set looks. I vividly remember the set designer, Tim Averill, explaining his concepts and inspiration for the look of the design on the first day of rehearsal. It was incredible to see these visions being executed so brilliantly!
        </p>
        <p>
         Tech for a show is always a long process. &ldquo;Spacing out&rdquo; the dance numbers on the stage can get daunting. This process, though, is one of my favorite times. The ensemble is very active in the first act of the show, so I did not get much down time during our first day of tech. It was difficult yesterday to keep going from 6:45 to 11, meticulously rehearsing numbers, because of the long day I had yesterday. I am an education major, and on Tuesdays and Thursdays I have early-morning fieldwork, then classes, then rehearsal. It is interesting to move among all these different worlds. I love everything I do, and that is my caffeine for getting through the day. The next week will be difficult, but I know that breathing and taking the time to be appreciative for the opportunities I have will get me through!
        </p>
        <p>
         Today we have a hair tutorial, as I sit here writing this blog with my hair (ridiculously) in pin curls &mdash; more soon!
        </p>
        <p>
         OCT. 24: TECH REHEARSAL
        </p>
        <p>
         The first day of extended tech rehearsal happened this morning and afternoon. It&rsquo;s amazing to see a show that we have been working so hard on for two months starting to come together. We started where we left off yesterday &mdash; right before the ending number. The first rehearsal today was short and sweet, lasting only from 10 to 12:30. Then my castmates and I rushed to the dining hall to stock up before our next chunk of rehearsal.
        </p>
        <p>
         Promptly at 1 we were asked to return to start teching the show &ndash; cue-to-cue. This means that we are primarily focusing on lighting. This can be a tedious part of the tech process, as there is a lot of fast-paced action, followed by stopping for long period of time, then going back to run the section &ldquo;full out&rdquo; (or with all the lighting cues in place). Sometimes it can take hours to do a section that, in real time can only take five minutes.
        </p>
        <p>
         During the cue-to-cue, we only got through about a half-hour of the show in real-time, but it took us four hours to cue. I do love this process because it shows me all of the aspects that come together in a magical way to make what I have been working on for months come to life. It feels wonderful to get this production lit in beautiful lighting, finally! Tomorrow is a tech rehearsal from 10 am to 11 pm. Though we do have a significant dinner break, I will be exhausted if I do not get some sleep tonight. I have to put down my lesson plans at some point and get some rest!
        </p>

        <h2>
         <a href="http://muhlenbergtheatredance.tumblr.com/post/132675082066/in-the-real-world-rehearsals-and-tech" title="In the&nbsp;&ldquo;Real World&rdquo;&hellip; Rehearsals and Tech">
          In the&nbsp;&ldquo;Real World&rdquo;&hellip; Rehearsals and Tech
         </a>
        </h2>
        <p>
         In many, many ways, Muhlenberg prepared me excellently for life in the rehearsal hall. The theatre I work for, Hedgerow Theatre (see earlier post for more!) is an SPT, meaning that we have an arrangement with Actor&rsquo;s Equity allowing us to use union actors in some (but not all) of our performances. &nbsp;This means we run by equity rules, much like Muhlenberg. &nbsp;Five&rsquo;s and Ten&rsquo;s are regular. &nbsp;Rehearsal is daily (Mondays off). And yes, 10 of 12 (10 hours of rehearsal in a 12-hour period) exists.
        </p>
        <p>
         What is different from rehearsals at Muhlenberg is that you don&rsquo;t know who you&rsquo;re working with or what you&rsquo;re working on before rehearsals start. &nbsp;When you walk into an audition you may not know the people on the other side of the table and the direction in which they&rsquo;re taking the project &ndash; also true of the first day of rehearsal. &nbsp; &nbsp;
        </p>
        <p>
         Typically, you get three weeks of rehearsal, one week of tech, previews and opening. &nbsp;At Hedgerow, we have two previews before an opening, whereas other theaters in the area, such as the Arden, have multiple weeks of previews. &nbsp;Rehearsals are also far less regularly scheduled than the mainstage rehearsals at Berg. &nbsp;It&rsquo;s much more like trying to schedule rehearsals in Directing Class and its midterms. &nbsp;Virtually everyone involved with a show has another job, so you may have an erratic rehearsal schedule.
        </p>
        <p>
         All this is to say &ndash; it gets a little less organized once you venture outside of the Bubble. &nbsp;To combat this be AS PREPARED AS POSSIBLE. &nbsp;The best approach to any rehearsal is to be ready to go as much as possible whenever possible. &nbsp;That means in your shoes and rehearsal jacket before the start of rehearsal with your lines off book and your body warmed up.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a>
         </a>
         Usually, everyone is on the same page and ready to work, which makes rehearsals as rewarding as possible. &nbsp;In the most recent show I rehearsed,
         <i>
          Dracula
         </i>
         , our director, the inimitable Dan Hodge, was acting in a production that opened the same week as we did, and so we had three hour morning rehearsals the week we opened. &nbsp;That&rsquo;s a small amount of time to get a lot of work done. We had to be on our game and ready as soon as it was time so that we were able to fit a run, notes and fixes in before the end of rehearsal.
        </p>
        <p>
         Working with such an amazing, professional and dedicated group of artists, as was the case with
         <i>
          Dracula,
         </i>
         was a real privilege. &nbsp;Amongst our numbers are &rsquo;06 graduate Meghan Winch and &rsquo;14 graduate Josh Portera (also a company member at Hedgerow). &nbsp;We were able to create a fantastic production with a limited rehearsal period due to the tenacity and readiness of everyone involved.
        </p>
