          <h1>
          </h1>
          <p align="center">
           <strong>
            Spring 2016 Schedule&nbsp;
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p class="DefaultCxSpMiddle">
           <strong>
            JANUARY
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p class="DefaultCxSpMiddle">
           <strong>
            <img src="/media/contentassets/images/about/cfe/Cholbi-edit-229x230.jpg" alt="Michael Cholbi" style="  padding : 10px;   float : right;" />
            Michael Cholbi lecture, &ldquo;Libertarian Paternalism: Manipulation without Coercion?&rdquo;
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p class="DefaultCxSpMiddle">
           <em>
            Jan. 26, Moyer Hall&rsquo;s Miller Forum, 6:45 p.m.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p class="DefaultCxSpLast">
           Libertarian paternalists such as Cass Sunstein favor approaches to policy that encourage individuals to choose in accordance with their own values or preferences without coercing them. Libertarian paternalism purports to promote individuals&rsquo; well-being while still respecting their autonomy or rational agency. Cholbi argues that Sunstein and others are correct in arguing that libertarian paternalism is not coercive but are incorrect about why: Coercion need not 'close off&rsquo; an individual&rsquo;s options. Rather, libertarian paternalism is not coercion because it communicates neither a threat nor an offer for its target to treat as a reason for acting. However, libertarian paternalism is a form of morally suspect manipulation, taking advantage of common weaknesses of reasoning and decision making.
          </p>
          <p>
           Cholbi is Professor of Philosophy at California State Polytechnic University, Pomona. He is an author and editor, and is the founder of the International Association for the Philosophy of Death and Dying.
          </p>
          <p class="DefaultCxSpFirst">
           <strong>
            FEBRUARY
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p class="DefaultCxSpMiddle">
           <strong>
            <img src="/media/contentassets/images/about/cfe/Epstein-edit-230x230.jpg" alt="Robert Epstein" style="  padding : 10px;   float : right;" />
            Robert Epstein lecture, &ldquo;The New Mind Control&rdquo;
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p class="DefaultCxSpMiddle">
           <em>
            Feb. 3, Seegers Union Event Space, 6:45 p.m.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p class="DefaultCxSpMiddle">
           High-technology (the Internet, in particular) is making possible new forms of mind control which are more powerful, subtle and widespread than any dystopian science fiction writer ever imagined. Works in this genre pictured flawed worlds in which the means of control were extreme and misfits suffered. In recent years, we have seen signs of new forms of mind control in the hands of a few corporations, which are unconstrained by government oversight or regulation; operate on a global scale; impact a wide range of attitudes, opinions, beliefs and behaviors; and are invisible to the people being controlled.
          </p>
          <p class="DefaultCxSpMiddle">
           Epstein is Senior Research Psychologist at the American Institute for Behavioral Research and Technology and a contributing editor for Scientific American Mind.
          </p>
          <p class="DefaultCxSpMiddle">
           <strong>
            <img src="/media/contentassets/images/about/cfe/MerchantsOfDoubt-edit-230x230.jpg" alt="Merchants of Doubt poster" style="  padding : 10px;   float : right;" />
            Documentary screening, &ldquo;Merchants of Doubt&rdquo;
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p class="DefaultCxSpMiddle">
           <em>
            Feb. 10, Moyer Hall&rsquo;s Miller Forum, 6:45 p.m.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p class="DefaultCxSpMiddle">
           Inspired by the book by Naomi Oreskes and Erik M. Conway, this documentary examines the history of corporate-financed public relations efforts to sow confusion and skepticism about scientific research.
          </p>
          <p class="DefaultCxSpMiddle">
           <strong>
            <img src="/media/contentassets/images/about/cfe/Potter-edit-229x230.jpg" alt="Wendell Potter" style="  padding : 10px;   float : right;" />
            Wendell Potter lecture
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p class="DefaultCxSpLast">
           <em>
            Feb. 17, Moyer Hall&rsquo;s Miller Forum, 6:45 p.m.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           Potter spent 20 years in corporate PR, and before retiring was the former head of corporate communications for Cigna. Since leaving this job he has become a vocal critic of the health care industry and its public relations tactics. He is a senior analyst at The Center
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            MARCH
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p class="DefaultCxSpMiddle">
           <strong>
            <img src="/media/contentassets/images/about/cfe/Offit-edit-230x230.jpg" alt="Paul Offit" style="  padding : 10px;   float : right;" />
            Paul Offit lecture, &ldquo;The Strange Case of Vaccine Exemptions&rdquo;
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p class="DefaultCxSpMiddle">
           <em>
            March 2, Moyer Hall&rsquo;s Miller Forum, 6:45 p.m.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p class="DefaultCxSpMiddle">
           Offit is the Director of the Vaccine Education Center at the Children&rsquo;s Hospital of Philadelphia (CHOP) and Professor of Vaccinology and Pediatrics at Penn. He has written books on the anti-vaccination movement, autism, alternative medicine and religion and science.
          </p>
          <p class="DefaultCxSpMiddle">
           <strong>
            <img src="/media/contentassets/images/about/cfe/Pickard-edit-230x230.jpg" alt="Victor Pickard" style="  padding : 10px;   float : right;" />
            Victor Pickard lecture, &ldquo;Media Democracy in an Age of Corporate Libertarianism&rdquo;
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p class="DefaultCxSpMiddle">
           <em>
            March 16, Moyer Hall&rsquo;s Miller Forum, 6:45 p.m.
           </em>
          </p>
          <p class="DefaultCxSpMiddle">
           The American media system is exceptional in many ways. It is a largely commercial system that is offset by weak public alternatives, it is lightly regulated and in many sectors and it is dominated by a few corporations. Other countries&rsquo; media systems rarely face all three of these structural problems. This &ldquo;American exceptionalism&rdquo; was not inevitable; it was vigorously contested at key historical moments, and resulted from specific policy battles, with commercial interests and values triumphing over others. Keeping this power arrangement intact today is a &ldquo;corporate libertarian&rdquo; ideology, in which the rights of media corporations are privileged over those of everyone else.
          </p>
          <p>
           Pickard is an Associate Professor at the Annenberg School for Communication where he researches the history and political economy of media institutions, media activism and the politics and normative foundations of media policy. He has co-edited and authored books on these topics.
          </p>
