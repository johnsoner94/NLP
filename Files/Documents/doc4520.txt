       <p>
        <a id="OREDU0000129" title="Muhlenberg College" href="/topic/education/colleges-universities/muhlenberg-college-OREDU0000129-topic.html">
         Muhlenberg College
        </a>
        President Peyton &quot;Randy&quot; Helm is stepping down from his post after leading the college for 12 years.
       </p>
       <p>
        Under Helm's tenure, the Allentown liberal arts college's financial position strengthened along with its academic profile. As the college's 11th president, he also oversaw a boom in campus expansion and construction.
       </p>
       <div class="trb_gptAd trb_ar_main_ad" data-role="adloader adloader_ad delayload delayload_done" data-adloader-size="300x250" data-state="adloader_unloaded" data-withinviewport-options="bottomOffset=100&amp;topOffset=1000000" data-adloader-position="articleCube" data-adloader-adtype="articlebottomcube" data-adloader-label="data-adloader-label">
       </div>
       <p>
        Helm, 66, will leave Allentown at the end of this month and retire to Maine. He recently discussed his time at the college and changes he's seen in students and higher education.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Q: How has Muhlenberg changed?
        </strong>
       </p>
       <div class="trb_ar_cont" data-ar-cont="Article continues below">
       </div>
       <aside class="trb_ar_sponsoredmod" data-adloader-networktype="yieldmo" data-role="delayload delayload_item" data-screen-size="mobile" data-withinviewport-options="bottomOffset=100&amp;topOffset=1000000" data-load-method="trb.vendor.yieldmo.init" data-load-type="method">
        <div id="ym_1000171">
        </div>
       </aside>
       <aside class="trb_ar_sponsoredmod trb_barker_mediaconductor" data-adloader-networktype="mediaconductor" data-role="delayload delayload_item" data-screen-size="desktop" data-withinviewport-options="bottomOffset=100" data-load-method="trb.vendor.mediaconductor.init" data-load-type="method" data-vendor-mc="data-vendor-mc">
       </aside>
       <p>
        <strong>
         A:
        </strong>
        I think in mostly good ways. The campus itself, the physical structure, has changed a lot. We've added to the science complex. We completed the addition of the sports facilities. A number of residence halls were torn down [and replaced]. &#8230; We really did a major expansion of Seegers Union and the dining hall. &#8230; I do think physically the campus is in better shape. I think we have strengthened the academic caliber of our students over the last 12 years. ... I think the faculty has embraced an emphasis on experiential learning. That means providing students with opportunities to take the theory they learn in the classroom and apply it to the real world. That could take the form of study abroad. &#8230; Experiential learning can take the form of internships. You go out to the real world and you actually work with a company or nonprofit or some other organization that is trying to accomplish something real and you take the theory you learned in the classroom and you put it in practice. &#8230; Ninety-four percent of our graduates within a year of graduation either have a job or they're in graduate or professional school. All this talk about liberal arts grads can't get job &#8212; not true here.
       </p>

          <a href="/topic/education/colleges-universities/muhlenberg-college-OREDU0000129-topic.html">
           Muhlenberg College
          </a>
          president Randy Helm reflects on his 12 years at school and issues facing higher education.

     <p>
      <strong>
       Q: Muhlenberg saw a construction boom and its endowment triple during rough economic times. How was that possible, especially with the Great Recession?
      </strong>
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       A:
      </strong>
      That was a scary time. We have always been financially very conservative and frugal, so we haven't been living beyond our means. We haven't been taking big gambles with our resources. When the Great Recession hit, did it have an effect on us? Yes, absolutely. But it didn't have the effect that it had on other institutions that were living closer to the edge financially. ... We had enrollment buffers, and we always hit our enrollment targets. That's the major risk for colleges like us. ... We have given raises since the recession every single year &#8230; and we didn't have any layoffs. We didn't cut benefits. And I'm very proud of that.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Q: How did you manage to be
      </strong>
      <strong>
       affordable for families?
      </strong>
     </p>
     <div class="trb_gptAd trb_ar_main_ad trb_ar_sntMobAd" data-role="delayload delayload_item" data-withinviewport-options="bottomOffset=100" data-load-method="trb.vendor.snt.init" data-adloader-position="sntbanner" data-adloader-adtype="sntbanner" data-load-type="method" data-adloader-label="data-adloader-label" data-adloader-size="300x250" data-v-snt="//content.synapsys.us/embeds/crime/dynamic_mobile/partner.js" data-screen-size="mobile">
     </div>
     <p>
      <strong>
       A:
      </strong>
      We are affordable, but we are also a significant investment for families, and we know that. We have tremendously increased the amount of financial aid we provide and we did a lot of that through fundraising. We raised $35 million to $40 million for financial aid. &#8230; We're [also] more diverse than we ever were. The ethnic diversity of the campus has greatly increased during the last 12 years. We had the most diverse freshman class that we ever had &#8212; the class of 2019. We had an admissions staff that has worked very hard. &#8230; We're over 20 percent students of color, underrepresented groups, international backgrounds.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Q: How have students and parents changed the last 12 years?
      </strong>
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       A:
      </strong>
      I think students are very focused on where they're going in life. One other thing that's happening nationally, and it's noticeable on our campus, I believe the current generation of students is more fragile. I think part of it is that as a nation, we have become better at diagnosing learning differences, emotional problems, and all sorts of challenges. &#8230; We've become better at not just flushing people out of the system and saying, 'You're not college material.' &#8230; You can aspire to go to college. Colleges now find there are a lot of young men and women on their campuses who need extra support services. [Parents] are more involved. And the students want them more involved. Between classes, you see students on their phone talking with parents&#8230;. [Parents are] not coming from a place to be annoying. Sometimes they have unreasonable expectations, and sometimes they have habits of intervening &#8230; but I always respect that their job is to be an advocate for their children.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Q: Muhlenberg has an optional SAT admissions policy. Do you think more colleges will move toward that?
      </strong>
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       A:
      </strong>
      We were one of the earlier schools to make [the SAT] optional. I think many more schools have done that and I think that's the right thing to do. Frankly, the SATs are not great predictors of college success. ... If you really want to provide access and opportunity, regardless of family background, the SATs do not help in that regard. They were developed to level the playing field, and they haven't leveled the playing field. I think there are many ways to evaluate a student's ability to thrive in college. The SATs aren't useless, but they certainly are not the silver bullet. Muhlenberg did the right thing. They were cutting edge.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Q: What do you see as some of the biggest problems facing higher education institutions?
      </strong>
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       A:
      </strong>
      Certainly the financial model is a problem. The financial problem is based mostly on tuition, especially for private, high-quality institutions like Muhlenberg. &#8230; We're already near the limit of what families can afford, no matter how much they value what we're doing. That is a huge challenge. We're addressing it by trying to keep costs down and raising endowment funds. &#8230; Other problems that face higher ed, I have to say that government regulation is becoming a bigger cost factor. This is at the state and federal level. I don't think people in government really understand how campuses work very well.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Q: When you took over, the relationship between the college and city was described as &quot;strained.&quot; Do you think it's improved?
      </strong>
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       A:
      </strong>
      It has improved. I think a college should be a good member of the community, and I think we are. Our students volunteer all over the place. &#8230; I'm not saying that our students are 100 percent angels, but I think 95 percent of them are, and the 5 percent aren't bad kids. I think students just forget they live in a neighborhood because they're young and they're learning. We try to get students to understand that and to be considerate.
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       Q: What will you miss the most about Muhlenberg?
      </strong>
     </p>
     <p>
      <strong>
       A
      </strong>
      : I will miss the people. They are family to me.
     </p>
