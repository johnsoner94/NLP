          <h3 class="intro-add02">
           Department News
          </h3>
          <p align="center" style="text-align: left;">
           <strong>
            &nbsp;Physics Majors and &Phi;&Beta;&Kappa;
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Benjamin N. Kimelman '16 and Alan Mendez &lsquo;17, both physics majors, were
           <strong>
            Phi Beta Kappa
           </strong>
           <strong>
            Inductees
           </strong>
           for 2016 and were honored on Sun, May 1 during the organization's induction ceremony.
          </p>
          <p align="center" style="text-align: left;">
           <strong>
            Physics Majors Recognized at 2016 Honors Convocation
            <br />
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p align="center" style="text-align: left;">
           <strong>
            Carlos J. Herrera Acevedo '17
           </strong>
           was awarded
           <strong>
            THE DR. ROBERT A. BOYER PRIZE.
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           The prize is awarded to a student who exemplifies the many outstanding qualities of Dr. Robert Boyer, Emeritus Professor of Physics, including sound scholarship, a genuine concern for others, adherence to high standards of ethical and moral principles and a deep commitment to the religious dimension of a liberal arts education.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Emran O. Lallow '17
           </strong>
           was a honored
           <strong>
            with THE CLIFFORD R. MOYER MEMORIAL PRIZE IN PHYSICS
           </strong>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           The prize is awarded to a student majoring in physics who displays exceptional qualities of scholarship, perseverance and commitment typified by Clifford Moyer.
          </p>

           <strong>
            &lsquo;Berg Students Look at the Sun with Help from the Lehigh Valley Amateur Astronomical Society
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p align="center" style="text-align: left;">
           <strong>
            <img src="/media/contentassets/images/academics/physics/astronomy/image.jpg" alt="" style="   margin : 0px auto; display: block; " />
            <br />
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           On September 15, 2015 &lsquo;Berg Students had a look at the Sun with the Help from the Lehigh Valley Amateur Astronomical Society.
          </p>
          <p>
           Sun spots and solar prominences are being viewed through special Solarscopes brought to campus by members of the Lehigh Valley Amateur Astronomical Society (
           <a href="http://www.lvaas.org">
            www.lvaas.org
           </a>
           ) . The telescope with a white light filter allows all wavelengths of light through but reduces the intensity to a safe viewing level so that students can view sunspots on the photosphere of the sun. The Hydrogen alpha scope only allows light at the wavelength of the Hydrogen alpha line at 656.2 nanometers for viewing the flares and prominences visible especially at the limb of the sun.
          </p>

           &lsquo;
           <strong>
            Berg Physics Cantalobber Team Wins Pneumatic Cantaloupe Launch Competition
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Muhlenberg&rsquo;s Cantalobber Team, composed of students, Alan Mendez &lsquo;17, Carlos Herrera Acevedo &lsquo;17, Emran Lallow &lsquo;17, Victor Lora &rsquo;17, Jarred Graber '18, Sarah Geisler '18 and Mohammad Tobolat &lsquo;18 participated in Kutztown University's Society of Physics Students 9th annual "Cantalobber" contest. The contest took place at noon on Sunday, April 19 on the north water tower field on the Kutztown University campus.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Our team participated in the pneumatic division and placed first, missing a target placed over 600 feet away by a mere 28 feet.
           </strong>
           Many supporter&rsquo;s from the Muhlenberg community came out to watch and support our victorious team.
          </p>
          <p align="center">
           <img src="/media/contentassets/images/academics/physics/Cantalobber.jpg" alt="" style="     " />
           <br />
           <span class="style1">
            From the left: Alan Mendez &rsquo;17, M. Jacob (advisor), Jarred Graber &rsquo;18, Sarah Geisler &rsquo;18, Mohammad Tobolat &rsquo;18, Carlos Herrera Acevedo &rsquo;17, Victor Lora &rsquo;17 and Emran Lallow &lsquo;17
           </span>
          </p>
          <p>
           Students and hobbyists alike built mechanical or pneumatic devices. In the mechanical division, teams competed to see which contraption could launch a cantaloupe the furthest over three launches, while pneumatic competitors, this year, strived for accuracy over three launches attempting to hit a target more than 600 feet away.
          </p>
          <p>
           Competitors in this year's contest included Kutztown University's Physics Club, Gettysburg College, Kutztown High School, Muhlenberg College, Chambersburg Area Senior High Physics Club, and Chink 58 (a team of boy scouts from New Jersey).

           <strong>
            The 2013 American Physical Society Division of Nuclear Physics Fall Meeting
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Research into the nature of nuclear matter under extreme conditions (and another new topical area) continues for faculty and students at Muhlenberg. Dr. Fadem and five Muhlenberg students (Benjamin Kimelman, Kathryn Kooistra, Julian Rutkowski, MariAnne Skolnik, and Cora Wallace) attended the American Physical Society Division of Nuclear Physics 2013 Fall meeting in Newport News, Virginia. Fadem presented a talk entitled,
           <em>
            Transverse Energy at Forward Rapidities at RHIC using the PHENIX Muon Piston Calorimeter
           </em>
           .
          </p>
          <p>
           Four of the students (Kimelman, Kooistra, Rutkowski, and Wallace) presented posters related to the transverse energy measurement. MarriAnne Skolnik presented a poster on research related to the possibility of heavy photons, a hot topic at this year's meeting. Marianne independently applied for and received a SULI Fellowship to work at Jefferson Labs.
          </p>
          <p>
           The posters sported the following titles:
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Detector response of the PHENIX Muon Piston Calorimeter for sqrt(S_NN) = 200 GeV Au+Au Collisions
           </em>
           , by Benjamin Kimelman
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Comparison of UrQMD, Hijing, and parameterized data as inputs to PHENIX detector simulations of RHIC Au+Au collisions in the forward/backward kinematic region
           </em>
           , by Kathryn Kooistra
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Study of the influence of neutral pion decay on the inflow and outlow of energy from the PHENIX Muon Piston Calorimeter
           </em>
           , by Julian Rutkowski
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            &nbsp;
           </em>
           <em>
            Calibrating the PHENIX Muon Piston Calorimeter for Au+Au Collisions at Sqrt(S_NN) = 200 GeV
           </em>
           , by Cora Wallace
          </p>
          <p>
           Support for this research included the National Science Foundation Research at Undergraduate Institutions grant to Muhlenberg College (grant number 1209240), and the Muhlenberg College Provost's Office.
           <em>
            &nbsp;
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            Light Yield Measurements of Heavy Photon Search (HPS) Muon Scintillator Hodoscopes
           </em>
           , by MariAnne Skolnik
          </p>
          <p>
           MariAnne's attendance and presentation at the conference was supported by SULI Fellowship and the Muhlenberg College Provost&rsquo;s office.
          </p>
          <p>
           <em>
            We'll all have to stay tuned to learn whether "dark" photons mediate interactions of the mysterious dark matter as well as the many other open physics topics discussed at the conference!
           </em>

          <p>
           Physics Department faculty Adam Clark and Jane Flood performed the induction ceremony at the Hoffman House on Muhlenberg College&rsquo;s Campus.
          </p>
          <p align="center">
           <img src="/media/contentassets/images/academics/physics/sps_induction.JPG" alt="" style="   margin : 0px auto; display: block; " />

          <p>
           &nbsp;

          <p>
           &nbsp;Founded in 1921, Sigma Pi Sigma is a member honor society of the Association of College Honor Societies. The society has some 75,000 historical members. Election to Sigma Pi Sigma is a lifetime membership.
          </p>
          <p>
           Sigma Pi Sigma exists to honor outstanding scholarship in physics; to encourage interest in physics among students at all levels; to promote an attitude of service of its members towards their fellow students, colleagues, and the public; to provide a fellowship of persons who have excelled in physics. Sigma Pi Sigma&rsquo;s mission is not completed in the induction ceremony with the recognition of academic accomplishment. In the four dimensions of Honor, Encouragement, Service, and Fellowship, the mission of Sigma Pi Sigma takes a longer view.
          </p>

           <strong>
            Astronomy Students Put the Sun in Clear View
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Muhlenberg Physics students in this semester&rsquo;s astronomy course had the opportunity to view solar prominences and sun spots thanks to members of the
           <a href="http://www.lvaas.org">
            Lehigh Valley Amateur Astronomical Society
           </a>
           .&nbsp; Each semester LVAAS members bring telescopes to campus at the invitation of Dr. Judith Parker, Lecturer of Physics, but the solarscopes were a first this semester.&nbsp;
          </p>
          <p align="left" style="text-align: center;">
           <img src="/media/contentassets/images/academics/physics/sunview1.JPG" alt="" style="   margin : 0px auto; display: block; " />
           <br />
           <span class="style1">
            Members of LVAAS and the Muhlenberg community view the sun.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p align="left">
           <br />
           Three tripods were set up in front of the &nbsp;Trumbower Science Building on Muhlenberg&rsquo;s campus.&nbsp; Each tripod supported two telescopes.&nbsp; The larger telescope had a &ldquo;white light filter&rdquo; mounted onto the front of the tube.&nbsp; While the filter appeared to be a sheet of ordinary tin foil, it is actually a metal coated polymer material made specifically for solar filters blocking out over 99.999% of the light making it safe for student viewing.&nbsp; Students were able to see sun spots on the sun&rsquo;s surface through this scope.&nbsp;&nbsp; The smaller scope is a dedicated solar telescope that provides a &ldquo;hydrogen-alpha light&rdquo; view of the sun.&nbsp; Students were able to observe solar prominences emitted from the sun.
           <br />
           &nbsp;
          </p>
          <div>
           71 students and their guests signed in and more than an equal number of faculty, staff and curious students passing by were eager to check out the sun.
          </div>

           <strong>
            NSF Awards Muhlenberg College a three-year Research at Undergraduate Institutions (NSF-RUI) award of $162,000
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Dr. Fadem of the Muhlenberg College Physics Department has earned his second NSF-RUI grant for research in experimental nuclear physics with Muhlenberg undergraduates.&nbsp; The grant, entitled &ldquo;RUI: Antiquarks and Ultra-Relativistic Heavy Ions at Muhlenberg College,&rdquo; allows the Department to continue a research program that includes the College in cutting edge research at Brookhaven National Laboratory with the PHENIX collaboration.&nbsp; PHENIX has designed, built, operated, and analyzed data from a large detector located on the 2.4 mile circumference ring of the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider.&nbsp; Every summer, Muhlenberg students have the opportunity to work shoulder to shoulder with some of the finest scientists in the world.&nbsp; Thanks to this new grant, this work will continue.
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Another Successful Summer at Brookhaven National Lab!
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Muhlenberg students just completed another summer of research at BNL.&nbsp; While past summers have been used to help with the construction of a new detector for the collaboration, this summer was used to focus on analysis of data taken by PHENIX at RHIC in 2010.&nbsp; Many of the scientists at BNL helped to make this among the most productive summers yet.&nbsp; Nine students worked at BNL this summer:&nbsp; Alexa Carollo, Nicole Cronin, Benjamin Schweid, MariAnne Skolnik, Matthew Slater. Andrew Tullo, Cora Wallace, Michael Young, and Christopher Zumberge.&nbsp;
          </p>
          <p>
           One exciting highlight of the summer was the after midnight trip to Columbia University in New York City to take part in the 3:00 a.m. simulcast announcing the discovery of the Higgs boson-like particle at the CERN LHC.
          </p>
          <p>
           At the end of the summer, the students returned to campus here at Muhlenberg to report on their progress as participants in Muhlenberg&rsquo;s summer science Friday series of talks by Muhlenberg undergraduates performing summer research.

           While there were many interesting talks at the conference, MariAnne says she most enjoyed the interactive talk given by the Graduate Student Panel. The panel and audience discussed the particular challenges women undergraduates face in transitioning from undergraduate to graduate education.
          </p>
          <p class="intro-add02">
           &nbsp;
          </p>
          <h3>
           Students
           <strong>
            Inducted Into Sigma Pi Sigma, The Physics Honor Society
           </strong>
          </h3>
          <p>
           Three students were inducted into Sigma Pi Sigma, the Physics Honor Society, on May 6th , 2011. Inducted were Jonathan Ben-Benjamin, Nicole Hammer and Lauren Ryan. The event took place at the
           <a href="http://www.davinci-center.org/">
            Da Vinci Science Center
           </a>
           in Allentown, PA. Members of the Physics Department faculty and staff performed the induction ceremony. Founded in 1921, Sigma Pi Sigma is a member honor society of the Association of College Honor Societies. The society has some 75,000 historical members. Election to Sigma Pi Sigma is a lifetime membership.
          </p>
          <p align="center" class="style1">
           <img src="/media/contentassets/images/academics/physics/SPSPHOTO1.JPG" alt="" style="   margin : 0px auto; display: block; " />
           Sigma Pi Sigma Inductees Lauren Ryan, Jonathan Ben-Benjamin and Nicole Hammer.
          </p>
          <p>
           Sigma Pi Sigma exists to honor outstanding scholarship in physics; to encourage interest in physics among students at all levels; to promote an attitude of service of its members towards their fellow students, colleagues, and the public; to provide a fellowship of persons who have excelled in physics. Sigma Pi Sigma&rsquo;s mission is not completed in the induction ceremony with the recognition of academic accomplishment. In the four dimensions of Honor, Encouragement, Service, and Fellowship, the mission of Sigma Pi Sigma takes a longer view.
          </p>
          <p>
           For more information concerning Sigma Pi Sigma please see this
           <a href="http://www.sigmapisigma.org/about.htm" target="_blank">
            link.
           </a>
          </p>
          <p class="intro-add02">
           &nbsp;
          </p>
          <p align="left">
           <strong>
            Physics Students Honored
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p align="left">
           Physics students Jonathan Ben-Benjamin, Class of 2011, and Nicole Harmmer , Class of 2011, each were honored with prizes at Muhlenberg's 2011 Honors Convocation.
          </p>
          <p align="left">
           Jonathan Ben-Benjamin was honored with the Dr. Robert A. Boyer Prize.
          </p>
          <p align="left">
           The Boyer Prize is awarded to a student who exemplifies the many outstanding qualities of Dr. Robert Boyer, Emeritus Professor of Physics, including sound scholarship, a genuine concern for others, adherence to high standards of ethical and moral principles and a deep commitment to the religious dimension of a liberal arts education.
          </p>
          <p align="left">
           Nicole Hammer was honored with the Clifford R. Moyer Memorial Prize In Physics.
          </p>
          <p align="left">
           The Clifford R. Moyer Memorial Prize In Physics is awarded to a student majoring in physics who displays exceptional qualities of scholarship, perseverance and
           <br />
           commitment typified by Clifford Moyer.
          </p>
          <p align="left">
           Each prize carries a monetary award.
          </p>
          <p align="left">
           <strong>
            Students Participate in Annual Cantalobber Contest
           </strong>
          </p>
          <p>
           Muhlenberg Physics students Andrew Nederlof, Ben Schweid, Steve Motchswiller, Nate Crossette, and Kyle Sullivan participated in Kutztown University&rsquo;s 5th Annual Catalobber Contest. Students Alex Isinhue and Matt Moscowitz helped build the &lsquo;Berg&rsquo;s trebuchet but didn't attend the contest.
          </p>
          <p align="center">
           <img src="/media/contentassets/images/academics/physics/clip_image002_000.jpg" alt="Physics" style="     " />
          </p>
          <p align="center" class="style1">
           Ben Schweid, Andrew Nederlof, Nate Crossette and Steve Motschwiller
          </p>
          <p align="center" class="style1">
           (Clockwise from noon)
          </p>
          <p>
           The contest was held at Kutztown University on Sunday, April 10th. Seven different teams from five different colleges and universities participated in the event. There were two classes of Cantalobbers, trebuchets and air cannons. Muhlenberg&rsquo;s entry was a floating arm trebuchet. They placed fourth in field of four with total summed yardage of 116 yards.&nbsp; The winning trebuchet had a total summed yardage of 660 yards.&nbsp;
          </p>
          <p>
           After getting a good look at the substantial competition, an example seen looming in the background of the photo below, the &lsquo;Berg team readily admits they have more work to do and plan on entering next year competition with a new and improved trebuchet.
          </p>
