            <h3 style="text-align: center;">
             <strong>
              Election 2016: Politcs as
              <em>
               Un
              </em>
              Usual&nbsp;
             </strong>
            </h3>
            <p>
             <a href="/media/contentassets/pdf/academics/polisci/2016 Election Series brochure - final083016.pdf">
              Elections Series Brochure
             </a>
             <strong>
              &zwnj;
              <br />
              <br />
             </strong>
            </p>
           </div>
           <p align="center">
            <strong>
             What Happened to the Vital Center? The 2016 Election and Fate of American Democracy
             <br />
             <a href="https://video.muhlenberg.edu/media/What+Happened+to+the+Vital+CenterF+The+2016+Election+and+Fate+of+American+Democracy+w++Sid+Milkis/1_50zouupb/23101781">
              click here to view video
             </a>
             <br />

           </p>
           <p align="center">
            <strong>
             <br />
             MISSION STATEMENT
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            The Muhlenberg Political Science Department takes seriously the mission of a liberal arts education: cultivating thoughtful, articulate, active, and responsible citizens. By introducing students to the tools of the discipline -- its theories, concepts and research methods &ndash; political science enables students to explore the power relationships, institutional arrangements, social-economic conditions and ideological commitments that shape public policy, political action and public argument in a diverse and globally interdependent society. The Department offers courses and independent and collaborative research experiences emphasizing the development of writing skills, critical thinking, and active and intelligent engagement with issues affecting contemporary public life.
           </p>
           <p align="center">
            <img src="/media/contentassets/images/academics/polisci/PSmagnet_000.jpg" alt="Political Science" style="     " />
           </p>
           <p align="left">
            <strong>
             DEPARTMENT PROGRAM GOALS AND OUTCOMES
             <br />
             (adopted 2008)
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Knowledge
            </strong>
            :
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             1. Students have a basic knowledge of the institutions, political culture, and processes of American politics. Political science graduates:
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Know
            </strong>
            and understand the relationships between the major institutions of American government at the national, state, and local levels;
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Are
             </strong>
             informed about and can meaningfully discuss contemporary political, social, and economic issues;
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Are
             </strong>
             familiar with the competing ideological and philosophical traditions that have nourished contemporary American political thought and the understanding of the rights and responsibilities of American citizenship;
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Understand
             </strong>
             the US policy making process, including the role of institutions, interest groups, political parties, media, and the public;
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Are
             </strong>
             knowledgeable about and can assess the institutions and processes that link individual citizens to their government, including social groups, political parties, public policy, elections and the media;
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Are
             </strong>
             acquainted with and can identify key national events, historical and contemporary political figures, social movements, individuals and groups that have shaped American political history.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <p>
            <strong>
             2. Students understand the common questions and methods that animate the study of political science as a discipline. Political Science graduates:
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Can
            </strong>
            identify and discuss the major subfields in the and are familiar with the central topics and major debates within those subfields;
           </p>
           <ul>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Know
             </strong>
             how politics both shapes and is shaped by social, cultural and economic forces, and by elites and institutional arrangements;
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Know
             </strong>
             key political scientists and how their theoretical arguments and approaches have contributed to the development of the discipline; and
            </li>
            <li>
             <strong>
              Are
             </strong>
             familiar with the leading journals in the discipline and with the American Political Science Association, its resources, standards of scholarship and purposes in the professional development of political scientists.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <p>
            <strong>
             3. Students know major factors contributing to globalization, its impact on international relations, and how different actors interpret, promote, and resist these changes. Political Science graduates:
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Recognize
            </strong>
            and apply the major theoretical and analytic frameworks for interpreting international relations;
            <br />
            <strong>
             Understand
            </strong>
            and evaluate critical events within and between other countries;
            <br />
            <strong>
             Know
            </strong>
            how political systems and sub-systems are connected through varying forms of asymmetric interdependencies;
            <br />
            <strong>
             Interpret
            </strong>
            globalization as an uneven and unequal process;
            <br />
            <strong>
             Know
            </strong>
            how international relations are mediated through international organizations and institutional arrangements; and
            <br />
            <strong>
             Recognize
            </strong>
            the role of dominant powers, including the U.S., in shaping international institutions, norms, and regimes.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             4. Students know how power is organized and exercised across political systems, and understand how political phenomena (including institutions, behavior, and ideas) vary across and within countries. Political Science graduates:
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Recognize
            </strong>
            and apply the major theoretical and analytic frameworks for conducting comparative political analysis;
            <br />
            <strong>
             Understand
            </strong>
            how institutional formats vary within and across political systems;
            <br />
            <strong>
             Understand
            </strong>
            how varying formal and informal institutional structures and public policies produce different sets of winners and losers.
            <br />
            <strong>
             Understand
            </strong>
            that other political systems may have solutions to public policy problems facing the United States.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             5. Students recognize and describe the major Western political ideas that shape political institutions, political movements, and policy debates. Political Science graduates:
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Identify
            </strong>
            major authors and texts that have shaped the Western tradition of political discourse;
            <br />
            <strong>
             Identify
            </strong>
            the basic ideas and values of major modern ideologies;
            <br />
            <strong>
             Describe
            </strong>
            how political ideas and worldviews shape, and are shaped by, economic and cultural values; and
            <br />
            <strong>
             Understand
            </strong>
            and articulate the basic principles and ideas underlying different conceptions of modern democracy.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Skills
            </strong>
            :
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             1. Students can write and speak clearly, thoughtfully, critically, &amp; persuasively about political, social, cultural, and economic phenomena.
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             2. Students can understand, interpret, analyze and synthesize information about political, social, cultural, and economic phenomena. Political Science graduates:
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Understand
            </strong>
            basic qualitative and quantitative research methods in the discipline, and their appropriate use in explaining political life;
            <br />
            <strong>
             Can
            </strong>
            identify, interpret, and use a variety of theoretical approaches and methods used to explain political life;
            <br />
            <strong>
             Can
            </strong>
            analyze and draw conclusions about political life based on sound reasoning, empirical research, scientific inquiry, primary and secondary analyses, and other approaches in the discipline; and
            <br />
            <strong>
             Know
            </strong>
            how to design research projects, to use appropriate citation methods, and to evaluate the uses and limits of different methods in research design.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             3. Students exhibit sound reasoning and deliberative skills in discourse; they are judicious in their evaluations of others&rsquo; arguments. Political Science graduates:
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Can
            </strong>
            discriminate between reasoned debate and punditry and recognize the relative value of each;
            <br />
            <strong>
             Know
            </strong>
            how to formulate reasoned arguments (oral and written) arguments about politics, supported by evidence; and
            <br />
            <strong>
             Can
            </strong>
            evaluate the merits of various sources of information underlying political arguments.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             4. Students can read diverse texts (legal, philosophical, polemical, and scholarly) critically and meaningfully.
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             5. Students are competent and efficacious in navigating the institutions and procedures of government.
             <br />
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Values and Commitments
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             1. Political science students are committed to engaged democratic citizenship. Political science graduates:
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Appreciate
            </strong>
            the diverse ways citizens practice democratic politics;
            <br />
            <strong>
             Value
            </strong>
            the rights and obligations of citizenship in a democracy;
            <br />
            <strong>
             Are
            </strong>
            interested in articulating and deepening the meaning of democracy&rsquo;s core values, including equality and liberty;
            <br />
            <strong>
             Respect
            </strong>
            political and partisan differences and appreciate the value of provocative debate;
            <br />
            <strong>
             Contribute
            </strong>
            to the ongoing debates regarding the meaning of democratic principles and practices, both in and outside the U.S.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             2. Students value diversity and difference as crucial to democratic life. Political science graduates:
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Understand
            </strong>
            the value of learning about, interacting with, and respecting diverse cultures and traditions;
            <br />
            <strong>
             Promote
            </strong>
            tolerance and respect for differences in public life.
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             3. Students are committed to academic integrity and uphold standards of professional conduct.
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p>
            <strong>
             4. Students value social scientific modes of inquiry.
            </strong>
           </p>
           <p align="justify">
