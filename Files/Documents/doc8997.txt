           <strong>
            Sharon Albert:
           </strong>
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            Cho, Francisca, and Richard K. Squier. &ldquo;&ldquo;He Blinded Me With Science&rdquo;: Science Chauvinism in the Study of Religion.&rdquo;
            <em>
             Journal of the American Academy of Religion
            </em>
            76.2 (2008): 420 -448. Web. &nbsp;
            <a href="http://0-jaar.oxfordjournals.org.library.muhlenberg.edu/content/76/2/420.full">
             http://0-jaar.oxfordjournals.org.library.muhlenberg.edu/content/76/2/420.full
            </a>
           </p>
           <blockquote>
            <p>
             This article questions the privileging of science and scientific method as primary criteria for legitimating other fields of study, and particularly the academic study of religion. The authors argue that a re-examination of the assumptions not only of religion but also of science itself &ldquo;actually suggests more similarities than differences,&rdquo; and will ultimately "render the academic study of religion [and of science] into . . . comparable 'research tradition[s].'&rdquo;
            </p>
           </blockquote>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Jim Bloom:
           </strong>
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            Uglow, Jenny. "The Other Side of Science."
            <em>
             New York Review of Books
            </em>
            57.11 (2010): 30-34. Print. 31 Aug. 2010.
            <a href="http://0-me9qj4aj8y.search.serialssolutions.com.library.muhlenberg.edu/log?L=ME9QJ4AJ8Y&amp;D=LQL&amp;J=THENEWYORRE&amp;U=http%3A//library.muhlenberg.edu/record=b1063257">
             Available in Trexler Library, A level, microfilm
            </a>
            .
           </p>
           <blockquote>
            <p>
             Everyone should read this because it persuasively makes the distinction so-called liberal learning rests on: the unattainable wisdom to which we ideally aspire as opposed to the easy pursuit of ephemeral information, the "expertise" and "authority" discussed in the article. Think of the near reverence with which we utter such phrases as "best practices" and "benchmarking."
            </p>
            <p>
             Everyone should read this because in considering "all forms of cultural performance" Uglow and Shapin evoke the fun, necessity, and difficulty sidestepped by another of our glibly bandied phrases "interdisciplinarity."&nbsp; After reading this article, you may want to substitute this infelicitous piece of&nbsp; shopworn academese for the earthier noun impurity.&nbsp; The impurity or sheer messiness that inquiry and learning require, though inevitably at odds with institutional efficiency and personal security, not only keep us in touch with physical matter and intangible ideas in all their recalcitrant complexity. Impurity also keeps us free and open to experience as the following observations, from different kinds of perspectives, indicate:
            </p>
            <ul>
             <li>
              "Cultural purity is an oxymoron. The odds are that, culturally speaking, you already live a cosmopolitan life, enriched by literature, art, and film that come from many places." Anthony Kwame Appiah,
              <em>
               Cosmopolitanism
              </em>
              (2007)
             </li>
             <li>
              "For Bohr, physics was not about finding out what nature is, but about what can be said about it. Quantum mechanics was a complete theory of the behavior of matter and light, and we just have to come to terms with the limitations it places on what can be known, for example as illustrated by the Heisenberg uncertainty principle. Einstein was having none of it. He believed that there is an objective world out there and that it is the job of scientists to describe it. The appearance of probabilities in the theory was, for him, evidence of its incompleteness." Review of
              <em>
               Quantum
              </em>
              &nbsp; by Manjit Kumar Quantum
              <em>
               New York Times Book Review
              </em>
              I 6-13-10.
             </li>
             <li>
              "Theron could not feel sure how much of the priest's discourse was in jest, how much in earnest. 'It seems to me,' he said, 'that as things are going, it doesn't look much as if the America of the future will trouble itself about any kind of a church. The march of science must very soon produce a universal skepticism. It is in the nature of human progress. What all intelligent men recognize to-day, the masses must surely come to see in time.'"&nbsp; Harold Fredric,
              <em>
               The Damnation of Theron Ware
              </em>
              (1896)
             </li>
            </ul>
           </blockquote>

          <h2>
           Science and Sensibility: Bruce Wightman's Reading List
          </h2>
         </div>
         <div class="roundbox innerbox innerbox_1" id="content18321645">
          <p>
           <em>
            Prof. Bruce Wightman has
           </em>
           <em>
            selected representative works from the four key speakers invited by the Center for Ethics for Fall 2010, plus some other of his favorite articles, all of which speak to issues of HOW science gets done by human beings (rather than the content of the science itself).
           </em>
          </p>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Douglas, Heather E.
            <em>
             Science, Policy, and the Value-Free Ideal
            </em>
            . Pittsburgh, Pa: University of Pittsburgh Press, 2009. Print.
           </strong>
           &nbsp;
           <a href="http://library.muhlenberg.edu/search%7ES0?/aDouglas,+Heather+E./adouglas+heather+e/-3,-1,0,B/frameset&amp;FF=adouglas+heather+e&amp;1,1,">
            Available in Trexler Library, Muhlenberg Rm call # 174.95 D734s
           </a>
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            Our first speaker, Douglas addresses the &ldquo;problem&rdquo; of bias in scientific thought with a focus on the influence of science on public policy and policy-makers. Douglas&rsquo;s book provides a very accessible philosophy of science, with an excellent history of the growth of the role of scientists in shaping public policy over the 20th century. In so doing, Douglas reveals the inherent tension among scientists who often want to be seen as outside politics, yet want their work to publicly funded and valued by society.
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Livio, Mario.
            <em>
             Is God a Mathematician?
            </em>
            New York: Simon and Schuster, 2010. Print.
           </strong>
           <a href="http://library.muhlenberg.edu/search%7ES0?/tis+god+a+mathematician/tis+god+a+mathematician/1,1,2,B/frameset&amp;FF=tis+god+a+mathematician&amp;1,,2">
            Available in Trexler Library, Muhlenberg Rm call # 510 L788i
           </a>
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            Mathematical arguments have great persuasiveness and in science the gold standard remains a mathematically-predictive model of a particular mechanism. Livio addresses the fundamental question of why math explains so much of the natural world. Is it because there is something natural about mathematics? Or is mathematics a construct of human beings that limits the kind of questions we ask? Livio&rsquo;s book includes an extensive review of the history of mathematics. Some readers have been confused by the fact that Livio doesn&rsquo;t answer the title question, entirely missing the point. There are, of course, massive philosophical and religious implications to asking the question in the first place.
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           Skloot, Rebecca.
           <em>
            The Immortal Life of Henrietta Lacks
           </em>
           . New York: Crown Publishers, 2010. Print. &nbsp;
           <a href="http://library.muhlenberg.edu/search%7ES0?/aSkloot,+Rebecca,+1972-/askloot+rebecca+1972/-3,-1,0,B/frameset&amp;FF=askloot+rebecca+1972&amp;1,1,">
            Available in Trexler Library, Muhlenberg Rm call # &nbsp;616.02774 S628
           </a>
           <a href="http://library.muhlenberg.edu/search%7ES0?/c616.02774+S628i/c616.02774+s628i/-3,-1,,E/browse">
            i
           </a>
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            Skloot&rsquo;s gripping history of the HeLa cell line and the family that provided it reads like a work of fiction even though it is the outcome of a decade of exhaustive and intensive journalistic research. Skloot takes us inside the mid-20th century ethic of medical research, where human beings, especially poor, disenfranchised human beings, were seen only as subjects to be studied. We learn about the unintended consequences of research on human subjects, how scientists&rsquo; infamous inability to communicate effectively with laypeople can have disastrous results, and confront America&rsquo;s horrifying history of Nazi-like research on retarded African-Americans. &nbsp;
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Cassidy, David C.
            <em>
             Beyond Uncertainty: Heisenberg, Quantum Physics, and Thebomb
            </em>
            . New York: Bellevue Literary Press, 2009. Print.
           </strong>
           <a href="http://library.muhlenberg.edu/search%7ES0?/acassidy,+david/acassidy+david/1,2,3,B/frameset&amp;FF=acassidy+david+c+1945&amp;1,,2">
            Available in Trexler Library, Muhlenberg Rm call # 530.092 C345b
           </a>
           &nbsp;
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            An engaging and readable work of scientific history, Cassidy takes us deep inside Heisenberg&rsquo;s participation in trying to help build a nuclear weapon for Nazi Germany. Cassidy addresses issues of moral responsibility of scientists and how guilt can mold how scientists contextualize their own work.
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Mayr, Ernst. &ldquo;Darwin's Influence on Modern Thought.&rdquo;
            <em>
             Scientific American
            </em>
            283.1 (2000): 78-83. Print. &nbsp;
           </strong>
           <a href="http://library.muhlenberg.edu/record=b1082538">
            Available in Trexler Library, Level C Periodicals
           </a>
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            Certainly among the greatest biologists, Harvard&rsquo;s Ernst Mayr discusses how 19th century Darwinian ideas framed the 20th century western zeitgeist. In this short article written for laypeople, Mayr explains how evolution and natural selection led to changes in how people think about themselves and about society. Among other things, he discusses how variation along a cline helps us to reject essentialism and (among other things) the racism that comes with it.
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Barbour, Ian G.
            <em>
             Religion and Science: Historical and Contemporary Issues
            </em>
            . 1st ed. San Francisco: HarperSanFrancisco, 1997. Print.
           </strong>
           &nbsp;
           <a href="http://library.muhlenberg.edu/search%7ES0?/abarbour/abarbour/1,27,45,B/frameset&amp;FF=abarbour+ian+g&amp;6,,10">
            Available in Trexler Library, Main Collection call # 291.175 B239r, 1997
           </a>
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            Barbour&rsquo;s work stands as one of the key books that define the field of science and religion studies. Barbour, a Carleton College physicist, provides a brilliant and sweeping examination of the implications of scientific thought for Christian theology. This is a must-read for anyone interested in a serious examination of how faith can be rationally informed by scientific understanding. Barbour remains a key voice in rejecting assumptions that science and religion are, and/or must be, in conflict with each other.
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Moore, John Alexander.
            <em>
             Science as a Way of Knowing: The Foundations of Modern Biology
            </em>
            . Cambridge, Mass: Harvard University Press, 1993. Print. &nbsp;
            <a href="http://library.muhlenberg.edu/search%7ES0?/c574.09+M822s/c574.09+m822s/-3,-1,0,E/frameset&amp;FF=c574.09+m822s&amp;1,1,">
             Available in Trexler Library, Muhlenberg Rm call # 574.09 M822s
            </a>
           </strong>
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            Moore&rsquo;s book traces the history of science with a layperson&rsquo;s attention to the distinctive philosophy that defines science as an enterprise. He shows how the &ldquo;scientific revolution&rdquo; of Bacon, Galileo and Newton developed over a slow historical process starting with Aristotle. The book uses generous specific examples to help explain how the scientific approach came to be, and by extension how it is different from other methods of inquiry. Moore avoids the trap of reducing science to a particular definitive methodology, without sacrificing the distinctiveness of science as a mode of inquiry.
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Gould, S. J., and R. C. Lewontin. &ldquo;The Spandrels of San Marco and the Panglossian Paradigm: A Critique of the Adaptationist Programme.&rdquo;
            <em>
             Proceedings of the Royal Society of London. Series B. Biological Sciences
            </em>
            205.1161 (1979): 581 -598. Web.
           </strong>
           &nbsp;
           <a href="http://rspb.royalsocietypublishing.org/content/205/1161/581.full.pdf+html">
            Available via library subscription at http://rspb.royalsocietypublishing.org/content/205/1161/581.full.pdf+html
           </a>
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            Not so much a paper
            <em>
             about
            </em>
            science, but one of my favorite examples of great science writing. Gould and Lewontin use highly effective metaphors from architecture and literature to deliver a resounding critique of &ldquo;adaptionists&rdquo;&mdash;biologists who assume that evolutionary adaptation must be the explanation for anything we observe. This paper still stands today as a key work in biological thought and brought the word &ldquo;spandrels&rdquo; into the scientific vernacular. It also demonstrates how scientists can effectively borrow ideas and examples from other disciplines.
           </p>
          </blockquote>
          <p>
           <strong>
            Kuhn, Thomas S.
            <em>
             The Structure of Scientific Revolutions
            </em>
            . 3rd ed. Chicago, IL: University of Chicago Press, 1996. Print. &nbsp;
           </strong>
           <a href="http://library.muhlenberg.edu/search%7ES0?/tstructure+of+scientific+revolutions/tstructure+of+scientific+revolutions/1,1,2,B/frameset&amp;FF=tstructure+of+scientific+revolutions&amp;2,,2">
            Available in Trexler Library, Muhlenberg Rm call # 501 K96s, 1996
           </a>
          </p>
          <blockquote>
           <p>
            A massively influential work on the history of science, Kuhn provides a framework for understanding how scientific thinking is guided by paradigms and how major breaks from the paradigm often come from scientific researchers whose training is in a different discipline.
           </p>
