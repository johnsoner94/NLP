                <b>
                 The Kids Are Alright (Muhlenberg Magazine, Winter '15)
                </b>
               </a>
               <br />
               History shows that the youth of any nation are shiftless and prone to sloth; but is that really true?
               <br />
               <a href="">
               </a>
               <p>
                <b>
                </b>
                <br />
                Fri, 23 Jan 2015 10:22:00 EST
               </p>
               <div class="content-add05">
                <p>
                 Well, if I thought I would solve all the world&rsquo;s problems &ndash; and Muhlenberg&rsquo;s &ndash; before&nbsp;retiring, I guess I was wrong.
                </p>
                <p>
                 There&rsquo;s still plenty left for people to worry about: terrorism, the economy, ethnic and religious prejudice, racial strife, Ebola, conflict in the Middle East, gridlock in Washington, global warming, carcinogens, college costs, Vladimir Putin, North Korea. . . . To quote from a song of my youth, &ldquo;The whole world is festering with unhappy souls,&rdquo;
                 <sup>
                  2
                 </sup>
                 or from one of our great contemporary philosophers, &ldquo;We seem to be made to suffer, it&rsquo;s our lot in life.&rdquo;
                 <sup>
                  3
                 </sup>
                </p>
                <p>
                 All this, of course, is bread and butter for the media, whose frantic alarums compete for our attention during every waking information- saturated hour.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Among our favorite and most venerable worries is the state of today&rsquo;s youth: their feck- lessness, fragility and general unfitness to take the reins of society. Of course, members of my generation are fine ones to talk about the shortcomings of the rising generation, we of (formerly) long hair, bellbottoms, flower power, free love, hallucinogens and over amplified, overblown, pretentious rock music. But like all the generations before us, we claim the right to despair of today&rsquo;s student generation.
                </p>
                <p>
                 This is a trope that goes back at least 4,000 years to the ancient Sumerians and Egyptians. An Egyptian scribe around 1700 B.C. despaired of his students: &ldquo;&hellip;thou forsakest writing!
                </p>
                <p>
                 Thou givest thyself up to pleasures. Thou goest from street to street where it smelleth of beer....&rdquo;
                 <sup>
                  4
                 </sup>
                 The fourth century A.D. philosopher Libanius complained that students disrupted his lectures by laughing, chatting about chariot-races, singing popular songs and &ldquo;picking their noses with both hands at once.&rdquo;
                 <sup>
                  5
                 </sup>
                </p>
                <p>
                 And do not think for a moment that the Enlightenment and the Industrial Age saw any improvement in the morals of the rising generation.
                </p>
                <p>
                 In 1695, Robert Russell wrote a book of advice for the young, in which he despaired:
                </p>
                <p>
                 &ldquo;I find by sad Experience how the Towns and Streets are filled with lewd wicked Children [who] curse and swear and call one another Nick-names&hellip;.&rdquo;
                 <sup>
                  6
                 </sup>
                </p>
                <p>
                 In 1790, the Reverend Enos Hitchcock complained that, &ldquo;The free access which many young people have to romances, novels, and plays has poisoned the mind and corrupted the morals of many a promising youth.&rdquo;
                 <sup>
                  7
                 </sup>
                </p>
                <p>
                 In 1816, the
                 <em>
                  Tim
                 </em>
                 <em>
                  es&nbsp;
                 </em>
                 <em>
                  o
                 </em>
                 <em>
                  f&nbsp;
                 </em>
                 <em>
                  L
                 </em>
                 <em>
                  o
                 </em>
                 <em>
                  nd
                 </em>
                 <em>
                  o
                 </em>
                 <em>
                  n&nbsp;
                 </em>
                 inveighed against &ldquo;The indecent foreign dance called the Waltz&hellip;.It is quite sufficient to cast one&rsquo;s eyes on the voluptuous intertwining of the limbs, and close compressure of the bodies&hellip;. We feel it a duty to warn every parent against exposing his daughter to so fatal a contagion.&rdquo;
                </p>
                <p>
                 In 1859,
                 <em>
                  S
                 </em>
                 <em>
                  c
                 </em>
                 <em>
                  i
                 </em>
                 <em>
                  e
                 </em>
                 <em>
                  ntifi
                 </em>
                 <em>
                  c&nbsp;
                 </em>
                 <em>
                  Am
                 </em>

                  er
                 </em>
                 <em>
                  i
                 </em>
                 <em>
                  ca
                 </em>
                 <em>
                  n&nbsp;
                 </em>
                 opined &ldquo;a pernicious excitement to learn and play chess has spread all over the country&hellip;. A mere amuse- ment of a very inferior character, which robs &nbsp;the mind of valuable time that might be devoted to nobler acquirements&hellip;.&rdquo;
                </p>
                <p>
                 And in 1915, the psychologist Gail Harrison admonished, &ldquo;We seem to have dropped into an age of entertaining, a breathless going from one sensation to another, whether it be mechanical toys for the five-year-old or moving-picture plays for the sixteen-year-old. It not only destroys their power to think, but also makes happiness, contentment, and resource- fulness impossible.&rdquo;
                 <sup>
                  8
                 </sup>
                </p>
                <p>
                 It would seem, then, that today&rsquo;s college students represent the latest (and therefore presumably the most degraded) manifestation of what the critic and literary theorist Northrop Frye once called &ldquo;The Great Western Butterslide&rdquo; &ndash; the common belief that western civilization has been steadily deteriorating since...well, since forever.
                 <sup>
                  9
                 </sup>
                </p>
                <p>
                 Frye, of course, was anticipated in this by the Greek poet Hesiod (around 700 B.C.) who described the history of the human race as one long downward spiral into selfishness, wickedness, and frivolous lawsuits.
                 <sup>
                  10
                 </sup>
                </p>
                <p>
                 One mathematically minded commentator has calculated that if each generation since the Sumerians has seen a 5% decline in virtue, then the current generation of students retains only three one hundred thousandths of the virtue of Sumerian youth. . . leaving them essentially devoid of any virtue whatsoever.
                 <sup>
                  11
                 </sup>
                </p>
                <p>
                 To all of which I reply, in the words of another great college president
                 <sup>
                  12
                 </sup>
                 , &ldquo;Horsefeathers!&rdquo;
                </p>
                <p>
                 If there is one thing I am knowledgeable about, it is college students. Yes, there are some who treat college as an expensive four- year bacchanalia, best enjoyed on &ldquo;the streets that smelleth of beer&rdquo; &ndash; but not many. And given the cost of tuition these days, parents are less tolerant of misspent opportunities than they used to be.
                </p>
                <p>
                 The vast majority of Muhlenberg students, the ones I know best, are an &nbsp;excellent bet to lead our society in the coming years. They are focused on the future: 96% are in graduate or professional school or are employed within one year of graduation. They take their studies seriously and are determined to get the most out of their four years at Muhlenberg: 34 percent double-major, many of them across disciplines. They are highly competitive applicants to law school and medical school (92% of Muhlenberg grads applying to medical school over the past six years have been accepted &ndash; more than double the national average). They are engaged citizens: 63% engage in some form of community service while at &rsquo;Berg. And, thanks to the wonders of Facebook (which allows me to keep up with our younger alumni), they land good jobs, marry, have kids, rise to leadership positions in their communities and support their alma mater. Most of them, in fact, are doing better in their first decade out of college than I ever did.
                </p>
                <p>
                 They are not sheep, they are not slackers, they are too intellectually tough and resilient to be ruined by waltzes, nicknames, chess, mechanical toys, movies, novels, or plays or by whatever else modern times will throw at them. As long as they continue to ask tough questions, challenge received wisdom, hone their analytical skills through the clash of ideas, make connections between ancient verities and new insights, they will do just fine.
                </p>
                <p>
                 So, that&rsquo;s one less thing for you to worry about.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Be well, and thanks for your friendship and support throughout 12 wonderful years.
                </p>
