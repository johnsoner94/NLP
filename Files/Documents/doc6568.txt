          <div class="content-add10">
           <h3>
            Daniel J. Wilson
            <a href="mailto:dwilson@muhlenberg.edu">
             <img src="/media/contentassets/images/academics/llc/faculty/envelope.gif" alt="Languages, Literatures, &amp; Cultures" style="     " />
            </a>
            &zwnj;
           </h3>
           <p>
            Professor of History
           </p>
           <ol>
            <li>
             Ettinger 306B
            </li>
            <li>
             484-664-3323
            </li>
           </ol>
           <p>
            <strong>
             Education:
            </strong>
           </p>
           B.A., The University of Wisconsin - Whitewater (1972)
           <br />
           M.A., The Johns Hopkins University (1974)
           <br />
           Ph.D., Johns Hopkins University (1976)
           <br />
           <br />
           <strong>
            Teaching Fields:
            <br />
           </strong>
           American Social, Cultural and Intellectual, 19th and 20th Centuries, History of Disease, Medicine, and Public Health
           <p>
            My graduate training was in the cultural and intellectual history of the United States.&nbsp; Since coming to Muhlenberg in 1978, I have taught a wide variety of topics under that general heading.&nbsp; I have taught numerous advanced courses including Nineteenth Century America, the Civil War and Reconstruction, Women's History, African-American History, Environmental History and the History of Medicine and Public Health.&nbsp; The topics of my Introductions to History have included coming of age in America, the city in American culture, and epidemics in American history.
           </p>
           <p>
            The courses I teach are generally organized on a discussion basis, although I will provide some background materials in lectures.&nbsp; Students are expected to read and be able to discuss both primary sources and sources written by historians.&nbsp; My courses also emphasize writing and historical research.&nbsp; To the extent possible, I require students to use the rich and ever growing collections of primary sources available on the Internet.
           </p>
           <p>
            Since the early 1990s I have focused my scholarly work on the history of the polio epidemics in the United States.&nbsp; My studies of polio have emphasized the experiences of the individuals who had the disease during the many epidemics prior to the development of the Salk polio vaccine in 1955.&nbsp; To date I have published three books and several articles on the subject.&nbsp; I am also particularly interested in the subject of masculinity and disability and have been active in disability studies.
           </p>
